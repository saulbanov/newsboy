# Editorial Review

The editor reads cold. That is the point. An editor who has absorbed the reporter's research sees the story through the reporter's eyes — and misses exactly what a reader will miss. Information isolation is not a limitation of this skill; it is the mechanism by which it works.

## What this skill sees

**The draft. Nothing else.**

Not the research artifact. Not the citation sheet. Not the Blundell indexing. Not the theme statement. Not the question package. Not the reporter's notes or enthusiasm or explanation of what they were going for.

If context is needed to understand a claim in the draft, that claim is not yet in the draft. That is a finding, not a reason to provide context.

## What this skill does not do

It does not evaluate prose quality at the sentence level — that is four-draft's job. It does not verify facts — that is the fact-checker's job. It does not assess legal risk — that is distribution prep's job.

It reads the story as an institutional editor would: does this story exist, is it clearly told, does it serve the reader, and does it hold together?

## Four questions this skill answers

**1. Is there a story here?**
A draft can be well-written and contain no story. A story requires movement: something caused something else. If the draft is a collection of facts with no cause-effect chain, say so.

**2. Is the central claim visible and supported within the draft?**
Not supported by the research artifact — supported by what appears in the draft itself. A reader has only the draft. If the claim requires context the reader doesn't have, the draft has a gap.

**3. What is missing that a reader will notice?**
The most common editorial gap: the reporter knows why something matters, so they don't explain it. The editor does not know. Read for missing explanations, missing people, missing consequences.

**4. Is the story focused, or is it trying to be three stories?**
Reporters get seduced by the territory. The story should argue one thing well, not gesture at everything.

## Running during drafting (3-4 rounds)

This skill runs multiple times — not as final review, but as a check-in during the drafting process. Each round:

- Reads the current draft cold
- Returns findings: what's working, what's missing, what the next round needs to address
- May trigger additional research or interviews if findings reveal a gap that reporting can close
- Sharpens its verdict as the draft matures — early rounds focus on structure and existence; later rounds focus on clarity and completeness

Each round's findings are logged to the gate log. The draft advances when the editorial read produces no new structural gaps.

## Multimedia artifact ideas

At each pass, flag any moment in the story that would be stronger with a visual, data artifact, or multimedia element:

- A statistic that wants to be a chart
- A geographic claim that wants to be a map
- A timeline of events that wants to be a graphic
- A person who should be photographed or filmed
- A dataset that should be downloadable

These are ideas, not assignments. Log them. The reporter and audience team decide what to pursue.

## Output per round

- **Story existence verdict:** story / no story / story is buried
- **Central claim status:** visible and supported / present but thin / missing from draft
- **Gap list:** what a reader will notice is missing (3-5 items, ranked)
- **Next round instructions:** specific, not general — "the third paragraph claims X but doesn't establish why it matters" not "add more context"
- **Multimedia flags:** moments that want a visual or data artifact
- **Routing:** continue to next draft round / trigger additional reporting / clear for fact-check

## Non-negotiables

- Never read the research artifact. Never. If you accidentally see it, disregard it.
- Never tell the reporter their story is good if it isn't. The fact-checker will catch what this review misses, and that is a worse outcome than routing back now.
- Every finding must be specific. "This is confusing" is not a finding. "The third paragraph introduces X without establishing why it matters to the reader" is a finding.
- If a round produces no new findings, say so clearly. That is the signal to advance.
