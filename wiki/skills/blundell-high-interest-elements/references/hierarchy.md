# The Reader-Interest Hierarchy

Blundell's ranking of story material by reader engagement. Lower-ranked material is not forbidden — it's necessary. The discipline is to use it sparingly and to substitute higher-ranked material wherever possible.

---

## 1. Built-in movement

Something is happening, it is having specific effects, and a counter-action may be underway. This is the highest-interest condition — not a type of material but a property of the story idea itself.

Stories with built-in movement can cover sins of execution. A reporter must do a superior job to make an analytical or static story interesting; an idea with action in it survives more writing mistakes.

When selecting among story ideas of roughly equal importance, choose the one with the most built-in movement. When assessing an idea, ask: what is happening, who is being affected, and is anyone fighting back? If you can't answer all three, the idea may lack the action that sustains reader interest through a long piece.

---

## 2. People/Actors

Actors are those pressing buttons, pulling levers, or getting ground up in the gears. They are not the uninvolved.

Two criteria:
- They must have direct involvement — either causing the development or directly experiencing it
- They must be doing or saying something interesting and germane

Actors have credibility because they have suffered or acted. The farmer whose land is being contaminated is more credible than the professor who studies contamination. The regulator who made the enforcement decision is more credible than the advocacy group that criticizes it.

Without actors, no matter how many people are in the story, there are no people. Including a forklift operator to "humanize" a warehouse inventory story isn't human interest — it's genuflecting to human interest without serving it.

---

## 3. Facts that move

Facts earn their place only when they are relevant *and* push the story forward. The test: if this fact were removed, would the story's argument or evidence be weaker? If not, cut it.

Irrelevant facts and facts of tangential worth are filler. Reporters who don't have enough action or direct human experience often try to cover the gap with accumulated facts. Readers recognize this and disengage.

---

## 4. People/Observers

Observers are analysts, consultants, researchers, lawyers, commentators — people who comment on what is happening from the outside. They have insight but not direct experience. They are useful; they are not actors.

The reader regards observers with coolness. They know the observer hasn't suffered, hasn't had skin in the game. An observer's opinion is less compelling than an actor's testimony.

Observers are necessary in most stories. But they should be used sparingly, and sources with direct experience should be given prominence instead. The most common failure: the reporter builds the story around observer quotes when actors were available but harder to reach.

---

## 5. Numbers

Big numbers are abstract. The mind pauses when it encounters them, trying to translate the data into something it can picture. If too many abstracts arrive simultaneously, the mind gives up.

Numbers stacked in consecutive paragraphs are the most reliably unread prose in journalism.

Use numbers when they're essential. Round them when precision doesn't matter. Translate them into pictures when they're important. See `references/numbers.md` for technique.
