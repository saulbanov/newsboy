# Actor vs. Observer

The most common and most damaging reader-interest failure in journalism. Most reporters know to include people. Few understand that the type of person determines whether the story convinces.

---

## The distinction

**Actor**: Directly involved. Either causing the development or directly experiencing it. Has pressed buttons, pulled levers, or been ground up in gears. Has suffered or acted.

**Observer**: Uninvolved. Comments on what others are doing or experiencing. Analysts, consultants, researchers, advocacy groups, academic experts, lawyers not party to the action.

The reader's response to each is different. Actors have credibility because they have direct knowledge and direct stakes. Observers have only opinion, however informed.

---

## The substitution failure

The most common version: a reporter builds a story around expert and observer quotes when actors were available but harder to reach. The expert is easy — a phone call and a credentialed opinion. The actor requires finding the right person, gaining access, and doing difficult reporting.

The result: a story full of people who haven't experienced the development commenting on people who have. The reader senses the distance and disengages.

Blundell's example: it is simply impossible to do a convincing piece on the root causes of Mexican migration without going to the villages of the people driven north and showing why they leave — yet reporters have repeatedly tried to do the impossible from a mountaintop.

---

## Diagnosing the substitution

Signs you've made the substitution:
- Most of your quotes come from people with titles: professor, director, spokesperson, analyst
- The people in your story are describing what other people experience rather than what they experience
- You have no scenes — moments of action observed directly — only testimony about action
- Your sources are in offices, not in the field

---

## The corrective

For every observer quote in a draft, ask: is there an actor who could say or show this instead?

Sometimes the observer is the right choice — when expert context is genuinely needed, or when the actor's testimony alone would be unclear without framing. But this should be a deliberate decision, not a default.

When actors are not available (they declined, they're dead, they're inaccessible), name the obstacle. A reporter who tried and couldn't get to the actor is doing better journalism than a reporter who didn't try. And acknowledging the obstacle is honest about the story's limits.

---

## A special case: the observer as actor

Some observers are actors in a secondary sense — they are directly involved in responding to the development even if they didn't cause it. A lawyer filing a lawsuit is an actor in the countermoves story. A regulator making an enforcement decision is an actor in the institutional response story. An advocacy group that has organized, picketed, and lobbied is an actor, not just an observer.

The test: are they doing something, or only saying something? If doing, treat them as actors.
