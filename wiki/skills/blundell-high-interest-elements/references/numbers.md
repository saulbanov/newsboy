# Handling Numbers

Numbers are necessary in most stories. The question is not whether to use them but how to use them without destroying reader engagement.

---

## The stacking rule

Never stack numbers in consecutive paragraphs. A paragraph dense with statistics followed immediately by another dense with statistics produces a wall of abstraction that readers stop reading.

If you have multiple sets of related numbers, separate them. Use quotes, observations, or illustrations between number passages. Give the reader's mind time to process one set before the next arrives.

---

## Round when precision doesn't matter

$2.6 million is cleaner than $2,611,423. If the exact figure isn't the point, the rounded one serves better.

Apply the same logic to percentages and proportions: "increased by more than a third" is clearer than "increased 36.7%." "Almost doubled" is clearer than "increased 98%." These are pictorial — they let the reader visualize a slice of pie, or two pies where there was one.

---

## Translate large numbers into ratios

Large numbers are abstract. The mind can't picture 14,654,231 drivers. It can picture one in four.

Use ratios to simplify: instead of "14,654,231 American drivers out of 58,013,261 own foreign cars," write "one in four American drivers owns a foreign auto."

The ratio is not less accurate — it's more communicable.

---

## Supply visual equivalents for important numbers

If a number is important enough that you can't omit or round it, give the reader a way to picture it.

A large number that must stay precise needs a companion — something familiar that conveys its scale. The farmland story example: "The prime land lost yearly would form a corridor a half-mile wide from San Francisco to New York." The acreage figure alone is abstract; the corridor is concrete.

Find the equivalent that the reader can see. The test: if I read this number aloud to someone, could they picture its scale? If not, find the picture.

---

## The density principle

When a set of related numbers is involved, decide exactly what you want to convey and build the simplest passage that does that one job.

Before: "Spending on redundancy research by the Office of Unessential Affairs rose from $847 million in fiscal 1983 to $1.26 billion this year, a 49% increase." — four numbers, high density.

After: "Over the past fiscal year the Office of Unessential Affairs increased spending on redundancy research by almost half, to $1.26 billion." — two numbers, lower density, same information, clearer emphasis.

The rewrite focuses on what matters (the scale and the change), drops the base year figure (not essential to the point), and converts the precise percentage to a pictorial fraction.

---

## Numbers and the features mindset

Reporters who come from breaking news bring a reflex trust in numbers — numbers define news, numbers are credibility. In feature writing, numbers per se do not possess magic. They are one element among many, ranked toward the bottom of the interest hierarchy, and must compete for space with material that carries more intrinsic reader interest.

The discipline: be choosy about which numbers earn a place, and careful about how they're treated when they do.
