# McPhee Source: Key Passages from "Draft No. 4" (The New Yorker, April 22, 2013)

These are the anchoring passages. Don't quote them in output — use them to inform the workflow.

---

## On Draft 1 and the Dear Mother method

McPhee's letter to a blocked student named Joel:

> Write "Dear Mother." Then tell your mother about the block, the frustration, the ineptitude.
> Go on like that as long as you can. Then delete the "Dear Mother" and all the whimpering
> and whining, and just keep the bear.

The operative lesson: writing begins only when something — anything — exists on the page.
"Until it exists, writing has not really begun."

---

## On the four-draft ratio

> The first draft of a long piece on California geology took two gloomy years; the second,
> third, and fourth drafts took about six months altogether. That four-to-one ratio in
> writing time—first draft versus the other drafts combined—has for me been consistent in
> projects of any length.

Use this to reframe the writer's frustration with Draft 1 slowness. It's supposed to be slow.

---

## On revision as the essence

> The difference between a common writer and an improviser on a stage is that writing can
> be revised. Actually, the essence of the process is revision.

---

## On Draft 2 (reading aloud)

McPhee doesn't name the drafts explicitly in the essay, but he describes reading the second
draft aloud and "removing the tin horns and radio static that I heard while reading."

The metaphor: tin horns = passages that sound like performance, not truth.
Radio static = interference with the signal.

---

## On Draft 4 (the mot juste hunt)

> You draw a box not only around any word that does not seem quite right but also around
> words that fulfill their assignment but seem to present an opportunity.

> Your destination is the dictionary. [...] The dictionary definitions of words you are
> trying to replace are far more likely to help you out than a scattershot wad from a
> thesaurus.

The logic: the thesaurus shows you neighbors. The dictionary shows you what the word
actually *means*, which leads you to the better word.

Example from the essay: Looking up "sparkle" → "See 'flash'" → flash/gleam/glance/glint/
sparkle/glitter/scintillate/coruscate/glimmer/shimmer → "shoot forth light" → the phrase
that ends up in the piece.

---

## On the interstitial time

Between drafts, the mind keeps working. From McPhee's letter to Jenny:

> You may be actually writing only two or three hours a day, but your mind, in one way or
> another, is working on it twenty-four hours a day—yes, while you sleep—but only if some
> sort of draft or earlier version already exists.

Use this to remind writers that the gap between drafts is not wasted time.
