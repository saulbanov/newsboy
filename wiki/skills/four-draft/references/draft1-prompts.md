# Draft 1 Prompts: Getting the Blocked Writer Moving

These are opening questions. Use them one at a time. The goal is not to plan the piece —
it's to locate the bear and get something on the page.

---

## The bear question

"What's the one thing you saw / heard / found that made you want to write this piece?
Don't describe the story. Just describe that thing."

This bypasses the anxiety of structure. Every piece has a bear — the image, scene, fact,
or moment that justifies the whole. Once the writer names it, they have something to write
toward.

---

## The "tell me" question (for journalists)

"If you were explaining this to a friend over coffee — someone smart but who doesn't know
the beat — what would you say first?"

Spoken language is almost always clearer than written first drafts. The answer to this
question is often Draft 1, paragraph 1.

---

## The stakes question (for investigative work)

"Who gets hurt if this story doesn't get told, and how?"

This is not for the piece itself — it's to reconnect the writer to the reason the piece
matters. Useful when the writer is in the research spiral and can't find the door out into
writing.

---

## The "worst version" unlock

"Write the piece as if it's terrible. Intentionally bad. Clichés allowed. Explain too
much. Just get through it."

Sometimes the internal critic is the only thing standing between the writer and a first
draft. Giving explicit permission to write badly removes the critic's veto.

---

## For the writer who has too much

"What would you cut if you could only keep 40% of what you have?"

Research-heavy writers (journalists, academics) often can't start because they don't know
what to leave out. Forcing a cut first can clarify what the piece actually is.

---

## Signs Draft 1 is done enough to move on

- The piece has a beginning, middle, and end — even if all three are rough
- The writer can say in one sentence what the piece is about
- The "bear" appears somewhere in the draft

Draft 1 does not need to be good. It needs to exist.
