# Draft 4: The Dictionary Method in Detail

This is the mot juste hunt. Work a passage at a time. Never the whole piece at once.

---

## The method

1. Read the passage. Draw a mental (or literal) box around any word that is:
   - Merely OK when something exact exists
   - Vague where a concrete word is possible
   - Polysyllabic where a simple word would do the same job better
   - An abstraction doing a physical word's work

2. Look up the boxed word in the dictionary — not the thesaurus.
   - The definition will often name the precise word you need, or tell you how this word
     differs from its neighbors
   - The thesaurus lists; the dictionary distinguishes

3. From the definition's synonyms (if any), identify 2-3 candidates.
   Read the differences between them. Pick the one whose *hue* matches the sentence.

4. If no better word exists, leave the original. "Stet."

---

## McPhee's examples to teach the method

**"Sparkle" → "shoots forth light"**

Looking up "sparkle" → "See 'flash'" → flash/gleam/glance/glint/sparkle/glitter/
scintillate/coruscate/glimmer/shimmer: "shoot forth light."
He changed the manuscript: "The reflection of the sun races through the trees and
shoots forth light from the water."

**"Sport" → "diversion of the field"**

Trying to name why someone would travel by canoe in a modern nation. "Sport" felt wrong.
Dictionary: "1. That which diverts, and makes mirth; pastime; diversion. 2. A diversion
of the field." He stopped there. The phrase entered the piece.

**"Assimilate" → "incorporated into its substance"**

Writing about mental change in Arctic Alaska. "Assimilate" was worse than the word it
replaced. Dictionary definition: "3. To incorporate into the substance of the appropriating
body." He used: "to be incorporated, in however small a measure, into its substance."

---

## Common Draft 4 traps

- **Thesaurus as crutch**: The thesaurus shows you the word's neighborhood. The dictionary
  shows you what it actually means. Use the thesaurus only to find the word you need to look
  up in the dictionary.

- **Polysyllabic fog**: Longer words often feel more "writerly" but do less work. When
  a simple word and a complex word mean the same thing, prefer the simple one.

- **Over-boxing**: Not every word needs a replacement. If the dictionary confirms the
  original word is right, stet it and move on.

- **Losing the voice**: Draft 4 is precision work, not a style transplant. The mot juste
  is the word that's exactly right *in this sentence, in this writer's voice*.

---

## Claude's role in Draft 4

For each boxed word or phrase the writer brings:

1. Give the dictionary's core definition (1-2 lines)
2. Name the synonyms the definition distinguishes, if any
3. Explain the distinction between 2-3 candidates in plain language
4. Let the writer choose — do not recommend unless asked
5. If no clearly better word exists, say so and move on

Do not: rewrite the sentence. Do not: replace words the writer didn't flag.
