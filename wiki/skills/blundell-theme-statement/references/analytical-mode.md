# Analytical Mode — Extracting a Theme Statement from Existing Material

Use this when the reporter pastes in notes, transcripts, a draft, or any pile of reporting. The goal is to read the material through Blundell's lens, surface the action chain that is actually there — which may differ from what the reporter thinks they have — and return a usable theme statement plus a gap list.

---

## Step 1: Read for movement, not content

Skim the material looking only for things that are *happening* — actions, decisions, events, changes, conflicts. Ignore explanations, background, statistics not tied to change, and quotes that don't describe action.

Make a quick list of the actions you find. This is the raw material for the chain.

---

## Step 2: Identify the central development

Which action is the engine — the one that caused the others? Name it in a single plain sentence.

If multiple candidates compete: which one, if it stopped tomorrow, would make the rest of the story irrelevant? That's the central development.

---

## Step 3: Sort the material

Map what the material actually supports into these categories. Be honest — only include what the material shows, not what you'd expect to find:

**History** — Does the development have roots in the past? Is it a clean break or a continuation?
Note: historical context is rarely clumped in one place — look for it sprinkled throughout. Don't force it; only note it if it materially explains why this is happening now.

**Scope** — How widespread, intense, or varied is the central development?
Note: is it documented with numbers, named examples, geographic range, or only asserted?

**Reasons** — Why is it happening? What forces are driving it?
Note: economic, political/legal, social, psychological? Which reasons are documented vs. speculated? Psychological/personality reasons are the most commonly absent.

**Impacts** — Who is being hurt or helped, and how?
Note: are the emotional responses of those affected present, or only their analytical opinions?

**Countermoves** — Who is fighting back, adapting, or exploiting the situation? What are they actually doing?
Note: distinguish between people talking about countermoves and people actually taking them. Talk is cheap.

**Futures** — Where is this heading?
Note: are projections from formal studies, informal opinion, or the reporter's own inference?

Return this sort as a brief structured summary — one or two lines per category, noting what's there and what's thin or missing. This is the foundation for the gap list.

---

## Step 4: Determine story stage

Is the central development itself news to the reader, or is it a known backdrop?

- If the material is heavy on scope and thin on impacts/countermoves, the story is likely juvenile — and may be under-reporting forward elements.
- If the material is rich in impacts and countermoves but thin on establishing the development, the reporter may be treating a juvenile story as mature — an editor problem waiting to happen.

See `references/juvenile-vs-mature.md` for mixed cases.

---

## Step 5: Draft the theme statement

Write 2-3 sentences that honestly describe what the material *actually shows*, not what the reporter hoped to find. If these diverge, flag it.

The statement should:
- Name the central development and treat it at the right level (news or given)
- Name the most important documented impact
- Name the countermove or reaction, if the material supports it

No details, no names, no numbers. Just movement.

---

## Step 6: Return the gap list

Based on the theme statement, identify what it claims that the current material can't yet support. Prioritize by consequence:

- **Dealbreakers** — claims the story can't make without this material (e.g., scope is asserted but one anecdote isn't scope)
- **Significant gaps** — material that would substantially strengthen the story (e.g., countermoves described by opponents, not shown in action)
- **Nice to have** — material that would add texture but isn't load-bearing (e.g., emotional responses from affected parties)

Keep the gap list short. Three to five items at most, ranked by importance. The reporter decides which gaps to close before filing.
