# Interactive Dialogue — Early-Stage Story Ideas

Use this when the reporter has an idea but little or no reporting in hand. The goal is to move them from vague concept to a fenced, reportable story with a working theme statement.

One question at a time. Let their answer drive what to probe next. Don't skip ahead.

---

## Know what you're watching for

These are the failure modes that kill stories before they start. Keep them in mind throughout the dialogue and flag them when you see them:

- **Topic, not story**: "Water rights in West Texas" is a subject. "Panhandle farmers are losing groundwater access faster than anyone reported" is a development. Push until you have the latter.
- **Condition, not movement**: "The oilfield waste problem is serious" describes a static state. Push for what is *changing*, *worsening*, or *being contested now*.
- **Advocacy leading facts**: "The industry is corrupt and consumers are suffering." Ask what is actually happening, to whom, documented how. Conclusions should follow evidence, not lead it.
- **Too many stories at once**: Reporter wants to tell five things. Help them name which is the spine and which are sidebars or future pieces.
- **Effects that haven't happened yet**: The cause-and-effect map will always extend further than the reporting can reach. Flag speculative elements so the reporter doesn't waste time on them.

---

## Stage 1: What is actually happening?

Ask them to describe the central development in one or two sentences — the thing that is occurring or has occurred. You want the event, not the angle or the moral.

If they give you a topic, push for a development. The test: does it have a verb that describes change, action, or movement?

If what they describe is a condition that has always existed, ask: what has *changed recently* or *emerged* that makes this a story now?

---

## Stage 2: Is this story juvenile or mature?

This is the most consequential judgment in story shaping. Don't ask the reporter to answer it directly — they often don't know. Determine it yourself from context.

**Juvenile**: The central development is essentially unreported. Readers don't know it's happening. The story's job is to establish and scope the development, with one generalized effect.

**Mature**: The development has been substantially reported. Readers know it exists. The story's job is to cover what hasn't been covered: downstream effects, countermoves, second-order consequences.

To figure out which you have, ask: "Has this been reported? If I searched right now, what would I find?" If the reporter is the first person telling this story, it's juvenile. If they're advancing a story that's already out, it's mature.

Getting this wrong wastes reporting time. A journalist treating a mature story as juvenile will spend weeks establishing facts an editor will cut as given.

---

## Stage 3: Cause-and-effect sketch

Say: "Walk me through what logically follows from this development." Push them to name:

1. The immediate effects — who or what is being hurt or helped, and how
2. The second-order effects — what those effects are causing
3. The countermoves — who is acting to fight back, adapt, or exploit the situation

You're not looking for exhaustive coverage. You're looking for the 2-3 most important, most reportable links in the chain.

Watch for: effects that haven't had time to materialize, effects that are pure speculation, and effects that may have been disrupted by outside forces. Flag these as reporting traps. They look like story elements but aren't there yet.

---

## Stage 4: Fence the story

The cause-and-effect map is always larger than any one story. Help the reporter identify which portion they can actually cover given their access, sources, and timeline.

Ask: "Which part of this chain is most important *and* most reportable by you specifically?"

The answer to that question is the story. Everything else is future stories. Helping the reporter let go of the rest is often the most valuable thing this dialogue does.

---

## Stage 5: Draft the theme statement

Now synthesize. Write a 2-3 sentence draft that captures:

- The central development (treated as news if juvenile; as a given if mature)
- Its most important documented consequence
- The reaction or countermove, if present and significant

Read it back. Then ask two questions:

1. "If you finished reporting and the story said exactly this — would that be worth publishing?" If no, find what's missing.
2. "What does this theme statement tell you to go report?" A good theme statement makes the reporting checklist obvious. If the reporter can't answer this question from the statement alone, the statement isn't specific enough yet.

Revise until both questions have satisfying answers.
