# Worked Examples

Annotated examples from Blundell's own work. The structural skeleton comes first because it's the most transferable lesson — the examples illustrate it, not the other way around.

---

## The skeleton

Every theme statement follows the same underlying shape:

**[Central development] → [most important consequence] → [reaction, if present]**

Not all three are always there. A juvenile story may have only development + one consequence. A very mature story may have only consequence + countermove, with the development implied. But movement — something causing something else — must always be present.

A theme statement with no action chain is a topic, not a story.

---

## Example 1: Juvenile story — doctor shortage

**Situation**: Reporter discovers widespread physician shortage that has barely been reported.

**Theme statement**:
> An acute shortage of physicians in many specialties is developing, and the quality and availability of medical care is being affected as a result.

**What it does**: Names the development as news ("is developing"), adds one generalized effect (quality of care), and stops. It doesn't try to cover malpractice, insurance rates, or medical schools — all of which appear on the logic map but are out of scope for this story at this stage.

**Reporting implication**: Prove the shortage exists, document which specialties and localities are hardest hit, show the effect on patient care. The scope section carries most of the weight.

**What it avoids**: Trying to cover the entire cause-and-effect chain. The reporter drew a fence and stayed inside it.

---

## Example 2: Mature story — same doctor shortage, advanced

**Situation**: The shortage and its general effects on care are now well known. The reporter has found a new forward development.

**Theme statement**:
> The deterioration of medical care resulting from the doctor shortage is generating malpractice judgments that are pushing insurance rates through the roof. Some insurers are abandoning the business, and many physicians, appalled by rising rates, are going without coverage or forming pools for self-insurance.

**What it does**: Treats the shortage and its general health-care effects as given — "resulting from the doctor shortage" is one phrase, not a section. Leads immediately with the unreported development (malpractice → insurance rates) and includes the countermove (physicians forming self-insurance pools).

**Reporting implication**: Document the insurance rate increases, find the physicians going without coverage, show the pools forming. Don't re-prove the shortage — one line acknowledging it is enough.

**What it avoids**: Re-establishing what everyone already knows. An editor would cut that material. Start where the news actually is.

---

## The contrast

Notice what changes between the two versions: the development shifts from foreground to background. In version 1, proving the shortage *is* the story. In version 2, the shortage is the reason the real story — a downstream financial crisis in medical insurance — is happening at all.

Same underlying event. Completely different story, depending on what's already been told.

## Example 3: Profile — big-tree loggers

Profile theme statements work differently. Instead of describing an action chain, they name the facets of the subject the story will focus on. Everything else gets cut.

**Situation**: Blundell is profiling big-tree loggers in the Pacific Northwest. He could write about economics, equipment, industry structure, culture, safety, history. He chooses two things only.

**Theme statement** (reconstructed from the story):
> Big-tree logging is among the most dangerous work in America, and the men who do it form a fraternity bound together by pride, tradition, and a collective embrace of that danger.

**What it does**: Names two facets — extreme danger and the social code of the fraternity. Everything in the story illuminates one or both. Material that doesn't serve either facet doesn't appear.

**Reporting implication**: Find scenes and quotes that show the danger directly (not just assert it) and that reveal the fraternal code in action — the hazing, the exclusions, the stoicism, the gallows humor. The test for every piece of reporting: does this show danger, fraternity, or both?

**What it avoids**: A sprawling portrait that tries to cover everything about loggers and ends up illuminating nothing. The fence is narrow by design.
