# Juvenile vs. Mature Stories

The most consequential judgment a reporter makes when shaping a story. Getting it wrong sends reporting in the wrong direction and produces copy editors have to save.

---

## Juvenile story

The central development itself is news — readers don't know it's happening.

**Theme statement emphasis**: Prove the development exists and scope it. Show how widespread, intense, or varied it is. One generalized effect is enough — there isn't room to develop effects fully when establishing the development is the main job.

**Reporting emphasis**: Documentation, numbers, breadth of examples, first-hand observation of the development itself.

**The risk**: Treating a development that's already been covered as if it's new. The reporter wastes time and space proving what the reader already knows, and buries any actual news deep in the piece.

---

## Mature story

The central development has been reported. Readers know it exists.

**Theme statement emphasis**: Treat the development as a given — a brief orienting mention, not a proof. Stress the forward elements: effects that haven't been covered, legal or financial consequences, reactions from affected parties, countermoves underway.

**Reporting emphasis**: Depth on downstream elements — the impacts and who bears them, what countermoves are actually being taken (not just discussed), and where things are headed.

**The risk**: Re-establishing what everyone already knows. An editor will cut all that material, leaving a story that starts on paragraph 14.

---

## How to determine which you have

Ask: if a well-informed reader picked this up, would the central development be news to them, or would they already know about it and be waiting to find out what happened next?

If you're still uncertain: search. What has already been published? What's been established? What remains unreported? The gap between what exists and what doesn't is where the story lives.

---

## Mixed cases — where most real stories are

**The development is juvenile but one effect is mature**: A new development is being reported for the first time, but one of its downstream effects has already been covered elsewhere. Establish the development fully; treat that specific effect as a given and go deeper on the others.

**The story flips mid-reporting**: A reporter starts with a juvenile story, but someone else publishes before they file. Now the development is known. The reporter needs to pivot the theme statement to a mature version — cut the scope-proving, stress the forward elements they alone have developed.

**Depth vs. breadth split**: The central development has been reported broadly but never deeply in a specific place, institution, or population. This is often treated as juvenile (it's new *here*) but should usually be framed as mature — acknowledge the broader story exists, then go deep on what no one has shown before.

**The countermove is bigger than the original story**: Sometimes the reaction to a development becomes more significant than the development itself. The theme statement should reflect the actual center of gravity — which may now be the countermove, with the original development as context.

In all mixed cases, the rule is the same: the theme statement should honestly reflect where the real news is, and treat everything else as given.
