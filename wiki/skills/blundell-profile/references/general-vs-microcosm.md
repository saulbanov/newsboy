# General vs. Microcosm Profiles

The distinction determines everything about what to look for in reporting. Getting it wrong means collecting the wrong material.

---

## General profile

The subject is chosen for its inherent interest — qualities that make it unique or unusual. A person who has done something no one else has done, an institution that operates differently from others of its kind, a place with a character unlike anywhere else.

**Reporting emphasis**: What makes this subject different? What actions or behaviors reveal those qualities? How have these qualities shaped the subject's fortunes?

**Theme statement emphasis**: The qualities of differentness. Two or three facets that explain why this subject is worth reading about and that will filter everything else out.

**Subject selection test**: Is there something genuinely unusual here? Would a well-informed reader find this subject interesting regardless of what it represents about a broader class? If yes, general profile.

---

## Microcosm profile

The subject is chosen for its typicality — its similarity to others in its class. The subject is a vehicle for a wider story. A family during a strike that represents all families affected. A town during an energy boom that represents all boom towns. A farmer in crisis that represents all farmers in that crisis.

**Reporting emphasis**: What does this subject share with others in its class? What characteristics and experiences are held in common? How does this subject's story illuminate the wider condition?

**Theme statement emphasis**: The qualities of typicality. What makes this subject a representative example of the larger phenomenon?

**Subject selection test**: Does the subject actually represent what the reporter needs it to represent? This requires vetting before committing.

---

## Vetting for microcosm profiles

The lizard-boot failure: a reporter flies to Kansas to find a farmer suffering from the removal of milo price supports. The source at the Farm Bureau promises chapter and verse. The reporter arrives at a pillared mansion with a Mercedes in the drive, a Cessna out back, and an owner whose milo losses are covered several times over by wheat, condos, and oil. This is suffering?

Goodbye, story.

Vetting questions before committing:
1. Does the subject actually experience what the story claims? (Don't rely on second-hand assurance.)
2. Is the subject's situation typical of others in its class, or exceptional?
3. Are there features of the subject's situation that would make it unrepresentative? (Wealth, connections, geography, timing)
4. Can the subject speak to the experience in a way that generalizes — or only to their own specific case?

Half the work of a microcosm profile is finding the right subject. There are risks even when much care is taken. The collective profile reduces this risk.

---

## The collective profile

Instead of betting on one subject, use a town, a small institution, or a community as the subject. The reporter is sure to find other examples within the collective that better fit the theme. Other aspects of the story — economic impact, civic mood, institutional response — can be brought in alongside the individual examples.

The collective profile offers more reporting room and more flexibility while preserving the intimacy that makes profiles work. It reduces catastrophic failure risk without sacrificing depth.

Use when: the individual subject pool is uncertain, the story requires multiple examples to be convincing, or the development affects a community rather than primarily individuals.
