# Profile Theme Statement

A profile theme statement works differently from a development story theme statement. It names facets, not an action chain.

---

## What it names

Two or three facets of the subject that the story will focus on. Not what is happening. Not what is causing what. What kind of thing this subject is — what qualities, conditions, or aspects the story will illuminate.

The facets determine what to report and what to cut. Everything in the story must illuminate at least one named facet. Material that doesn't is cut, however interesting it might be in isolation.

---

## Examples

**Loggers** (general profile): "The extreme danger of big-tree logging, and the fraternal code of the men who do it."

Two facets: danger and fraternity. Not economics, not equipment, not history, not the industry's future — all of which exist in the territory but none of which illuminate these two facets. The theme statement is the fence.

**Cowboy** (general profile, paraphrased): "The life and work of a real cowboy in an age of cowboy hype."

One facet stated as a contrast: the reality of ranch work set against the romantic mythology. Everything in the story shows the gap between them — the cold, the danger, the low pay, the modern equipment, the absence of the cowboy life from popular imagination.

---

## How to write it

For a **general profile**: Name the two or three qualities of differentness that make the subject worth writing about. Ask: why is this subject unusual? What would reveal that unusualness in action?

For a **microcosm profile**: Name the two or three qualities of typicality that make the subject representative of its class. Ask: what does this subject share with others like it? What experience or condition does it embody?

The statement should be one sentence. It should be concrete — not "complexity and resilience" but "the gap between what loggers earn and the danger they face, and the code that makes them accept that bargain."

---

## How to use it

The profile theme statement serves two functions:

**As a reporting filter**: Before each interview or observation, ask whether what you're about to pursue illuminates a named facet. If not, note it briefly and move on. Don't report exhaustively on a subject — report specifically on the named facets.

**As a writing filter**: Before including any material in the draft, ask whether it illuminates a named facet. If not, cut it regardless of how interesting it is. The profile theme statement is a strict filter, stricter than the development story version — because the profile form inherently invites sprawl and digression.

---

## Difference from development theme statement

A development theme statement describes action: "X is happening, causing Y, and Z is fighting back."

A profile theme statement describes a subject's nature: "This subject is characterized by [facet 1] and [facet 2]."

The development statement can be used directly in the story as the nut graf. The profile statement usually cannot — it's a working tool for the reporter, not language for the reader. The story must show the facets through action and scene rather than naming them directly.
