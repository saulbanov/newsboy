# The Windy Bore Problem

The most common profile failure mode: a subject who is charming, garrulous, and fascinating in person becomes a talky bore in print.

---

## Why it happens

Profile reporters spend days with their subjects. The subject is often articulate, quotable, and generous with their time. The reporter fills notebooks with observations and quotes. They sit down to write and produce a piece in which the subject talks — at length, colorfully, about everything.

The reader meets a windy bore.

The problem is the reporter was seduced by the person and forgot to look for the action. A subject who speaks well is not the same as a subject who does interesting things. Quotes, however well-turned, are digressions. Without action to attach them to, they float free and the story drifts.

---

## The profile guide's corrective

The profile variant of the six-part guide addresses this directly in the Countermoves section: "Show me in action where possible."

This phrase applies beyond countermoves to the whole profile. Every section of a profile should be hunting for action — moments when the subject is doing something, deciding something, responding to something — not merely saying something.

The Values and Standards section (which replaces Reasons in the profile guide) is especially vulnerable to the windy bore failure. It's easy to fill this section with the subject's articulate statements of belief. The discipline is to find the *actions* that reveal those beliefs — what the subject does when their values are tested, not what they say their values are.

---

## Diagnosing it in a draft

Signs your profile has the windy bore problem:
- Most paragraphs contain quotes; few contain scenes
- The subject is the primary source for most of the story's claims about themselves
- You can describe what the subject believes but not show it in action
- The story feels like an extended interview rather than an observed portrait
- Readers who haven't met the subject would not find them interesting

---

## The fix

Return to the facet-based theme statement. For each named facet, ask: where in the story is this facet *shown* rather than *asserted*?

If the facet is only asserted — the subject says they are [quality], observers say they are [quality] — the evidence is thin. Find the scene where the quality is visible in action.

For each significant quote in the profile, ask whether it could be replaced or supported by a scene showing the same thing. Quotes should clinch or extend what scenes have already established — not carry the argument themselves.

The digress rule applies here too: the action is the river; the quotes are the still water. Keep the reader moving.
