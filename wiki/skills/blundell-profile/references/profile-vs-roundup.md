# Profile vs. Roundup

The approach should be determined by the nature of the development, not the reporter's comfort zone. Many reporters default to profiles because they're easier to execute; many others default to roundups because they're safer. The story should choose.

---

## The profile approach

**Strengths**:
- Involves the reader deeply through a single concrete subject
- Resolves complexities and abstractions into pictorial examples the reader can see and grasp
- The reader can get to know a subject in a way that's impossible in a roundup
- Easier to organize — the subject provides a built-in thread

**Fatal weakness**: A dull or unrepresentative subject. A boring subject cannot be made interesting by artful writing. A misrepresentative subject produces a story that doesn't exist.

**Use when**:
- The development is best understood through close-up observation of a single case
- The subject is complex enough that scattered roundup examples couldn't resolve it
- You have confirmed access to a subject who is both representative and interesting
- The story requires the reader to feel something, not just understand something

---

## The roundup approach

**Strengths**:
- Maximum reporting room — less risk of catastrophic subject failure
- Faster-paced, newsy flavor — can move from case to case in rapid succession
- More convincing for developments that are genuinely widespread
- Better suited when the story has a variety of effects and counteractions

**Fatal weakness**: Repetitiveness. If a development hasn't generated varied responses and reactions, moving from example to example produces a monotone list.

**Use when**:
- The development is broad-based and documented in multiple locations
- The story has variety — different types of effects, different constituencies responding differently
- Countermoves are underway in multiple places (a roundup of reactive material is far stronger than a roundup of passive suffering)
- No single subject could embody the full range of what the story needs to show

---

## The decision

Ask two questions:
1. Has the development generated enough varied reaction and response to fuel a roundup without repetition?
2. Is there a subject available who is both genuinely representative and genuinely interesting?

If yes to both: either approach can work; choose based on what the story most needs.
If yes to 1 but no to 2: roundup.
If no to 1 but yes to 2: profile or collective profile.
If no to both: the story may not be ready to report yet.

---

## The lazy profile trap

Some reporters reflexively use profiles because they're easier to execute than roundups. A few days with one subject produces copy. A roundup requires building sources across multiple locations.

The test: is this story best understood through one case, or does it require breadth to be convincing? If breadth is needed, the profile approach is a convenience choice, not a story choice — and it will show in the result.
