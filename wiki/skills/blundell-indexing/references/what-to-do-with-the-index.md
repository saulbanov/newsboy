# What to Do With the Index

The index is not the end. It's the bridge between reporting and writing. Three things to do once it's complete.

---

## 1. Read the category weights

Scan all six categories. Which have many entries? Which have few or none?

The categories with heavy entries are the story's spine — they'll get the most space in the piece. Categories with thin entries either need more reporting or belong only as brief mentions. Categories with no entries either don't apply to this story (fine) or represent reporting gaps (not fine — figure out which).

This is also when you confirm whether the story is juvenile or mature. A heavy Scope section and thin Impacts/Countermoves = juvenile. A thin Scope and heavy Impacts/Countermoves = mature. If the index reveals the wrong balance for how you've been thinking about the story, now is the time to correct course — before you've written anything.

---

## 2. Set section order

The index tells you what you have. Your theme statement tells you what matters most. Together they determine the order of sections.

The section with the most emphasis in your theme statement is almost always the first section you write in the body. After that, let the writing suggest the next section rather than pre-planning the whole order. The index makes this safe — you know what you have, so you can move between sections fluidly without losing track of material.

History is the exception: don't write it as a block. Sprinkle historical fragments wherever they provide contrast, authenticity, or context for what's happening now. Look for the T-codes and D-codes under History in your index when you need a contrast point, not when you're looking for a section to write.

---

## 3. Write off the index

Write your draft using the index as your primary reference. When you need a quote about scope, find the Scope section of your index and pick the entry you want. When you need an illustration for countermoves, find the Ill. entries under Countermoves.

Return to original interview notes or transcripts only to verify exact wording before publication. Don't re-read your full notes while drafting — that breaks the creative flow and usually pulls you toward material that isn't needed.

---

## The spontaneity benefit

The index preserves spontaneity. When the writing is going well, you don't break the spell fumbling through a pile of papers for a half-remembered figure. The index puts the material within reach without forcing you to re-excavate your sources.

Blundell: "Writing off the index maintains spontaneity. When the work is going well and the Muse is whispering the right words into my ear, I don't want to break the enchantment."

---

## Hand off to narrative lines

Once you know your category weights and have confirmed your theme statement, you're ready to choose a narrative line. Run `blundell-narrative-lines` with your theme statement and a brief description of what your heaviest categories are. The narrative line determines how the sections flow together into a story.
