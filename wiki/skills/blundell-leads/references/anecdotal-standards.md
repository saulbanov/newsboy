# Standards for Anecdotal Leads

Four standards. An anecdotal lead that fails any one of them should be reconsidered. A lead that fails two or more should be replaced with a general lead.

---

## 1. Simplicity

The illustration used must be clearly and immediately understandable. The reader has no investment yet and will not work to understand a complex setup.

Test: can the anecdote be hooked to what follows in a few words or, at most, a simple sentence or two? If the hook requires more than brief explanation, the anecdote is too complex for a lead.

Good: A pile of grain on one farmer's property that the government wants to add to. The connection to an agricultural payment program requires only one explanatory phrase.

Bad: A situation requiring a paragraph of context before the reader understands why it matters. Save that for the body, where the reader has enough investment to reason it through.

---

## 2. Theme Relevance

The example must align with the story's direction, not contradict it.

The most common failure: a vivid, well-written anecdote that would be a perfect lead for a different story — but this story goes the other way.

The Cuban example: an old man clutching a portrait of the pre-Castro dictator would be a perfect lead for a story about exiles who still dream of returning home. It was a terrible lead for a story about Cubans who were for the first time cutting their emotional ties to the island and settling in as Americans.

When the lead contradicts the story's direction, the reporter must confess the deception and shift the reader 180 degrees. Readers don't like being misled. The misused material belongs somewhere else in the story — usually as a perspective element in the scope section, or at the end.

---

## 3. Intrinsic Interest

The material used must be inherently interesting. The form of the anecdotal lead does not by itself create interest. Dull people doing predictable things make worse leads than a clear general summary.

Bad: "When the plant closed, 'everybody was worried,' says Dean Hanson, who has slaughtered cattle there for 25 years." This is an acute case of the blahs. The quote is unexceptional and predictable. Hanson's view lacks intrinsic interest.

The test: does this specific person, doing or saying this specific thing, compel attention on its own? If the answer is no, don't trust the anecdotal form to rescue it. Choose a general lead instead.

---

## 4. Focus

The example should illustrate a key section of the story — one that will be treated heavily and early in the body. The lead conditions the reader to expect development of whatever it introduces.

If the lead introduces material that gets only brief treatment in the body, or no treatment at all, the reader will accuse the story of false advertising.

Flexibility: if the best available anecdote comes from a secondary section but is vivid enough to grab the reader, use it — provided it slides easily into the main theme statement. Intrinsic interest outranks focus. A faithful-but-dull lead commits the most mortal offense: it doesn't make the reader read on.

---

## The fruit-salad lead

The failure mode that combines multiple problems at once: scraps of detail and anecdotal material drawn from different parts of the story tossed together in one confusing paragraph.

Addis Ababa — From the glass-walled towers of this modern capital... across the arid plains... to the jagged hills of the Southwest and the yurts of the Boogie Woogie tribe, where chieftain Abu Blu arises each morning to urinate out his tent flap to see which way the wind is blowing...

Everything but tomatoes and anchovies is in it, but there's still no clue what it means. Fruit-salad leads signal that the reporter hasn't committed to a story. The fix is to commit — pick one scene, one person, one moment — and lead with that.
