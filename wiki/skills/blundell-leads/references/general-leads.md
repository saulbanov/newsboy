# General and Summary Leads

The general lead is harder to write than an anecdotal one but often the better choice. It propels the reader into the story's heart more quickly and conserves focus-points-and-people material for the body.

---

## When to use a general lead

- The story has solid news value — use the hard-news lead variant and go for the throat
- Anecdotal material available is weak, dull, or fails the four standards
- The story's best scenes and actors should be preserved for the body, not spent on the lead
- The story is complex enough that a single anecdote can't carry its themes

The anecdotal lead is overused. Many reporters choose it automatically on features, assuming it will grab the reader. But a dull anecdote produces a worse lead than a clear, punchy general summary.

---

## How to build a general lead from the theme statement

After writing the body: take your refined theme statement and retool it as a lead.

Two moves to make it work:
1. Adopt a breezier or more dramatic tone than in the original theme statement
2. Deliberately omit one or more important elements — creating a mystery that forces the reader forward

The omission is what makes the lead work. The reader has been told enough to understand what the story is about but not enough to stop reading.

---

## The mystery principle

Two types of mystery work in general leads:

**Omit the why**: Tell the reader what happened but not why. The Yavapai Indians won their fight against the Orme Dam — they told the government to get lost and refused $33 million. But *why* would anyone refuse $33 million? The reader must continue to find out.

**Omit the how**: Tell the reader the situation but not the mechanism. A new breed of expert will help you figure out what to do with your free time — for a fee. What exactly is a "leisure consultant"? The reader must continue to learn.

The mystery must be genuine, not artificial. Withholding information the reader needs to understand the lead (rather than information that explains it) produces confusion, not intrigue.

---

## The minicomputer example

"Here come the minicomputers — and, right behind them, here come the crooks."

Twelve words. The main elements are identified and the connection between them is implied. Enough mystery remains (what are the crooks doing? how?) to push the reader forward. This is the general lead at its most efficient.

---

## The hard-news variant

When the development qualifies as genuine breaking news — something compellingly important is happening right now — use a blunt hard-news lead. Don't bury the news in an anecdote or a clever twist.

"One of the biggest scandals in the history of the insurance industry is beginning to break around Equity Funding Corp."

Not pretty, but urgent. The urgency is the point. An anecdotal lead would have buried the news; a clever general lead would have obscured its importance.

The test: if an editor knew only what this lead tells them, would they immediately understand why this story is important? If yes, the hard-news lead is right. If not, more context is needed before the urgency can land.

---

## When blocked on the lead

Write the body first.

The right brain delivers leads unbidden while the left brain works through the body's prose. Forcing the lead first, when the right brain hasn't had material to work with, produces labored results.

Once the body is written, the germ of a good lead is often already embedded in the refined theme statement. Retool it, apply the mystery principle, and the lead usually comes.
