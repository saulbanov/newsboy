# Laws of Progressive Reader Involvement

Blundell's framework for understanding what the reader demands at each stage of the story. Every structural decision — lead type, nut graf placement, section order, ending — should be evaluated against these four stages.

---

## Stage One: Tease me

"Intrigue me a little. Give me a reason for going on with your story instead of doing something else. Remember, I have no investment in you at all."

This is the lead's entire job. Nothing else. The lead does not prove anything, explain anything, or tell the whole story. It earns the reader's willingness to continue.

The lead fails Stage One if:
- It is dull (the reader has no reason to go on)
- It is confusing (the reader can't follow it)
- It is misleading (the reader feels deceived when the story goes a different direction)

---

## Stage Two: Tell me what you're up to

"Enough teasing. What is your story really about? Please, no windy explanations, no details — just what's going on here."

This is the nut graf's job. The nut graf delivers the main theme statement — a clear, simple expression of what the story is about, written in plain language. It satisfies the reader's demand to know what they've committed to.

The nut graf fails Stage Two if:
- It takes too long to arrive (the reader loses patience)
- It's buried in detail and explanation (the reader can't find the main point)
- It doesn't match what the lead implied (the reader feels cheated)

---

## Stage Three: I'm from Missouri

"You'll have to prove what you just said. Let's see your logic. Let's see your evidence. I've invested time in you by now, so I'll be patient — but you had better be convincing."

This is the story body's job. The body delivers the evidence — the illustrations, quotes, figures, and observations that make the reader believe what the theme statement claimed.

The body fails Stage Three if:
- Evidence is weak, thin, or based on observers instead of actors
- Logic is unclear or jumps without support
- Numbers are stacked without illustration
- The story makes claims it can't back up

---

## Stage Four: Help me remember

"I'll buy it. Help me remember it. Make it clear. Make it forceful. And put an ending on it that will nail it into my memory."

This is the ending's job. The ending fixes the story in memory — not by adding new important facts (which belong in the body) but by returning to the central theme in a way that makes it stick.

Good endings circle back to the lead's imagery, look ahead to the future in a way that carries the story's weight, or spread the lens suddenly to show the story's larger significance. They are expendable as facts but essential as impressions.

---

## How the stages govern structural decisions

**Lead type choice**: Anecdotal leads are best at Stage One when the anecdote is vivid, simple, and on-theme. General leads skip Stage One's specific scene and move more quickly to Stage Two — useful when the story's newsworthiness is its primary hook.

**Nut graf placement**: The reader demands Stage Two relatively quickly. Leads that delay the nut graf beyond three or four paragraphs are usually making the reader wait too long. The investment isn't there yet.

**Section order**: The sections should deliver Stage Three evidence in order of importance — most important proof first, supporting evidence following.

**Ending**: Expendable as fact; essential as impression. The ending exists to complete Stage Four, not to deliver material that belongs in the body.
