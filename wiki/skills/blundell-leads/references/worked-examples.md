# Worked Examples — Leads

Annotated leads from Blundell's own stories, showing the principles applied.

---

## The Yavapai Indians (general lead, mystery principle)

> The 360 Yavapai Indians on this small reservation, the shrunken remnant of thousands who once lived on 10 million Arizona acres, have won their first great victory over the white man. He wanted to stuff some $33 million into their pockets. They told him to get lost.

**What it does**: The first sentence establishes who the Yavapai are and that they have won something — surprising given their history of losses. The second and third sentences identify the mystery: why would anyone refuse $33 million?

**The omission**: Why they refused. The reader must continue to find out. The *why* drives the entire story — it's the spiritual and cultural connection to the land, the history of white promises broken, the Kakakas — none of which can be in the lead.

**Built from the theme statement**: This lead is, as Blundell notes, almost verbatim the theme statement he wrote at the start — with two items deliberately omitted. The why of the refusal, and the significance of the tribal vote, are withheld to create the mystery.

## The minicomputers (general lead, stripped down)

> Here come the minicomputers — and, right behind them, here come the crooks.

**What it does**: Twelve words. Identifies the two main elements and implies the connection between them. Leaves open the *how* — how are the crooks exploiting the minicomputer proliferation? The reader must continue.

**Why it works**: It has energy and rhythm. It names both parts of the story's equation. It creates enough mystery (what exactly are the crooks doing?) to pull the reader forward without being coy or confusing.

## The PIK farm program (anecdotal — simplicity at its best)

> Piled up on Joe Thompson's farm is the bulk of two years' corn harvests — 270,000 bushels in all, enough to keep the whole country in corn flakes for two weeks. The government wants to give him more.

**What it does**: The image is immediately understandable — a physical pile of grain, a concrete quantity translated into something familiar (corn flakes). The punchline (the government wants to give him more) delivers the absurdity of the PIK program in one sentence.

**Why it works on all four standards**: Simplicity — immediate and clear. Theme relevance — the program's absurdity is the story. Intrinsic interest — the image is striking. Focus — the scope section follows immediately and builds on Strano and Thompson's situation.

**What it didn't need**: A tie-in explaining what PIK stands for. The illustration was so perfect it required only a definition of PIK, which the writer would have needed anyway.

## The soccer lead (anecdotal — building to a shock)

> On a nippy, cloudy morn awhile back, one Julio Marchesan, captain of a local Ecuadorean-American soccer team, was watching his boys battle an Armenian team. Things were going badly for the Armenians, and in a spasm of frustration one of their players suddenly assaulted Mr. Marchesan — and bit his nose off.

**What it does**: Sets up the scene with deliberate calm and leisure, then delivers a shocking punchline. The calm setup makes the shock worse.

**The technique**: The leisurely approach ("a nippy, cloudy morn awhile back") creates a false sense of ordinary scene-setting. The reader is unprepared. This is the setup-and-reveal structure at its most effective in a lead.
