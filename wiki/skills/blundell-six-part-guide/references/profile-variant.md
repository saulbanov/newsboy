# Profile Variant

The profile version of the six-part guide replaces Scope with Qualities and Reasons with Values/Standards. The frame shifts from "what is happening" to "what kind of thing is this."

See `references/six-part-questions.md` for the full question set.

---

## The key divergences

**Qualities (replaces Scope)**
In a development story, Scope asks how widespread and intense the development is. In a profile, the equivalent question is: what makes this subject worth profiling?

For a **general profile**, the answer is *differentness* — the qualities that make the subject unique or unusual. The emphasis goes on these qualities. Everything in the story should illuminate them.

For a **microcosm profile**, the answer is *typicalness* — the ways the subject resembles others in its class. The subject is a vehicle for a wider story; its representative qualities matter, not its exceptional ones.

This determines what to look for in reporting: for general, find what sets the subject apart; for microcosm, find what makes it stand for others.

**Values and Standards (replaces Reasons)**
In a development story, Reasons asks why the development is happening. In a profile, the equivalent is: what does the subject believe in, and how does that shape its actions?

This is often the deepest level of a profile — the values that explain behavior that might otherwise seem arbitrary or inconsistent.

---

## Action emphasis in profiles

The profile guide stresses action and reaction more explicitly than the standard guide because the failure mode is different. Development story reporters tend to under-report effects and countermoves. Profile reporters tend to over-report what the subject *says* at the expense of what the subject *does*.

The countermoves section in the profile guide says "show me in action where possible" — a reminder that a charming and garrulous subject who only talks becomes a windy bore in print. Every quoted observation should be matched with something the subject has actually done.

---

## Profile theme statement

A profile theme statement names two or three facets of the subject that the story will focus on. It does not describe an action chain. It functions as a filter: everything in the story must illuminate at least one of the named facets. Material that doesn't is cut.

Example (loggers): "The extreme danger of big-tree logging, and the fraternal code of the men who do it."

This statement told Blundell to look for evidence of danger (not just assert it) and evidence of the fraternal code in action. It told him what to leave out: economics, equipment, industry structure, history — all present in the territory but none illuminating the two named facets.
