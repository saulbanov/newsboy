# Countermoves Guidance

Countermoves is the last action element in the six-part guide and one of the most commonly misreported sections. The central distinction is simple: talk is cheap, action precious.

---

## The talk/action distinction

A countermoves section built entirely on what critics, advocates, and opponents are *saying* is almost worthless. Readers absorb criticism of a development as background noise. What compels attention is when someone is actually *doing* something about it — filing a lawsuit, organizing a community, changing a regulation, building an alternative.

When reporting countermoves, weight what is being *done* far above what is being *said*. If the only countermove material is griping and handwringing, note that briefly and move on. Don't inflate a thin section with complaints.

---

## When countermoves appear

Countermoves is present only in a mature story. A juvenile story — one where the central development itself is news — may have no countermoves yet. The development is still new enough that organized response hasn't formed.

Don't force countermoves into a juvenile story where they don't exist. A sentence acknowledging that "response is still forming" is honest. Inventing or inflating countermoves to fill the section is not.

As a story matures, countermoves become increasingly important. In a fully mature story, the countermove may itself be the most newsworthy element — the original development treated as given, the response now the story.

---

## How to report countermoves

The questions to answer:
1. What is actually being done? Name the action, not just the intent.
2. Who is doing it? Name the actor, not just the category.
3. What is the scope? Apply the same scope dimensions as the development itself: how widespread, how intense, where?
4. How is it working? This is often the most neglected sub-question. A countermove that isn't working is still news.

The test for a countermove entry: can you describe it as a scene? If someone is fighting back, there should be a moment where you can show them fighting back — a court filing, a meeting, a confrontation, a decision. If there's no scene, you probably have talk, not action.

---

## The "lazy guru" trap

Blundell's warning: a lazy editorial consultant could build a reputation by scrawling "less talk, more action; fewer opinions, more facts" on almost every piece of copy without reading it, and playing golf the rest of the day. The criticisms would be correct.

This applies to countermoves specifically. Most reporters include the section. Few fill it with action rather than talk. The discipline is to notice the difference and not accept talk as a substitute.
