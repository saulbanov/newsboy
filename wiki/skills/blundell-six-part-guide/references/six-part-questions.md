# Six-Part Questions

All sub-questions from Blundell's framework, formatted for direct use. These are the actual questions — use them as a checklist when running planning or diagnostic mode.

---

## Standard guide (non-profile stories)

### I. History
A. Does the main theme development have roots in the past? What are they?
B. Is it a clean break with the past? How?
C. Is it clearly a continuation of the past? How?
D. Are there historical details that can lend authenticity and interest? Can they be related briefly?

*Note*: History is rarely the story's main emphasis. Its value is in contrast — showing what has changed and what hasn't. Use it as seasoning, not a section.

### II. Scope
**Quantitative factor**
A1. Can the scope be partially defined with numbers or other expressions of quantity? What numbers would be most meaningful?
A2. Can it be defined with comment and observation?

**Locale factor**
B1. What is the physical range of the development? International, national, regional, local?
B2. Where are the hot spots?

**Diversity/intensity factor**
C1. In how many different ways is the development likely to show itself? To what degree are people, places, and institutions involved?
C2. Is the development waxing or waning, spreading or contracting?

**Perspective factor**
D1. Do other developments bear on this one? Do they magnify its importance or temper it?

### III. Reasons
A. Economic: Is there money in this? Where does the money trail begin and end?
B. Social: Are changes in culture, custom, morals, or family life affecting this story? How?
C. Political/Legal: Are changes in laws, regulations, or taxes affecting this story? How?
D. Psychological: Are ego, vengeance, or wish fulfillment major driving forces? Does the personality of a major actor bear heavily on it?

### IV. Impacts
A. Who or what is likely to be helped? How? What is the scope of that help?
B. Who or what is likely to be hurt? How? What is the scope of the damage?
C. What is the emotional response of those helped or hurt?

*Note on C*: Get this by asking how they *feel*, not just what they *think*. These are different questions and produce different answers.

### V. Countermoves
A. Who is likely to gripe loudest? What are they saying?
B. What is actually being done to offset, combat, change, or deflect the impacts? What is the scope of this effort?
C. How is this effort working out?

*Note*: Weight B and C far above A. Talk without action is low-value. See `references/countermoves-guidance.md`.

### VI. Futures
A. Are there formal studies or projections that address the future? What do they say?
B. What are the informal opinions of both observers and actors? How do the latter see their own futures?
C. What does the reporting suggest the future might hold?

*Note on C*: Indicate, don't conclude. The reporter can present material that suggests what may happen; they have no business drawing flat conclusions.

## Profile variant

### I. History
A. How has the subject's past shaped the nature of him/her/it today?

### II. Qualities (replaces Scope)
A. What are the qualities of *differentness* — personal, professional, or other — that make the subject worth writing about? *(Emphasize for general profiles)*
   1. What kind of actions or behavior would reveal these qualities?
   2. How have these qualities affected the subject's fortunes and life?
B. What qualities of *typicalness* does the subject have? How is it like others in its class? *(Emphasize for microcosm profiles)*
   1. Do they share certain characteristics? Which ones?
   2. Do they share experiences? Which ones?

### III. Values and Standards (replaces Reasons)
A. What does the subject believe in most strongly? How does this shape actions in striving toward goals?
B. Are these beliefs different from or similar to those held by others in the same class? In what ways?
C. Where did these values, standards, and goals come from?

### IV. Impact
A. How does the subject affect those around it or other members of its class? Positives and negatives.
B. How is the subject being affected by circumstances or those around it? Positives and negatives.

### V. Countermoves
A. How are others actually responding to the subject's actions and attitudes? Show in action where possible.
B. How is the subject responding to circumstances and those around it? Show in action.

### VI. Futures
A. What does the subject think its future looks like?
B. What do others think it looks like?
