# Reasons Guidance

The Reasons section is the most neglected of the six. Reporters absorbed in story action forget to ask why something is happening. Of the four sub-categories, the psychological/emotional one is neglected most often.

---

## Why reasons gets skipped

Action is visible and concrete. Reasons are often hidden, require inference, and are harder to document. It's easier to report what is happening than why.

The cost of skipping reasons: the story explains what without explaining why, leaving the reader with an incomplete picture of the forces at work. For accountability journalism especially, the "why" often points directly to who benefits and who made a choice.

---

## The four reason types

**Economic**: Follow the money. Who profits from the current situation? Who would lose if it changed? The money trail often explains institutional behavior that otherwise seems irrational.

**Social/cultural**: Changes in customs, family structure, community norms, or generational expectations. Often a background force that shaped the conditions for the development without being its direct cause.

**Political/legal**: Changes in laws, regulations, enforcement priorities, or tax structures. For regulatory beat reporting, this is often the most proximate reason — the rule changed, or wasn't enforced, or was written with exceptions that benefited specific parties.

**Psychological/personality**: The hardest to get but sometimes the entire story. When an action doesn't make sense on its face given the actor's apparent interests, look for ego, vengeance, ambition, fear, or personal history driving it.

---

## Finding psychological motives

The tell: an action that defies logic given the actor's stated interests. Company A spends lavishly acquiring Company B despite an already overextended balance sheet and a poor strategic fit. The financial analysis doesn't explain it. The CEO's personal history and ego might.

How to pursue it:
- Ask sources: "Why do you think they really did this?" Let them speculate, then probe.
- Look for prior relationships between key actors — rivalries, partnerships, slights.
- Ask what success would look like personally for the key actor, not just institutionally.
- Read the actor's public statements over time for recurring themes, obsessions, or resentments.

This material is difficult to obtain and even harder to publish responsibly. But when it exists, it often reframes the entire story.

---

## Reasons and the accountability frame

For investigative work, the reasons section is where the accountability lives. The development may be the story; the reason it happened — who decided, who benefited, who was supposed to stop it but didn't — is the accountability story inside the story.

Ask not just why the development happened, but who had the power to prevent it and chose not to use it, and why they made that choice.
