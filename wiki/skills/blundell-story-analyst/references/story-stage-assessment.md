# Story Stage Assessment

Determining whether a published story is juvenile or mature changes what gaps are expected and which are damning.

---

## Reading stage from the text

**Signals of a juvenile story**:
- Lead establishes the development as news ("For the first time...", "A new study shows...", "Previously unreported...")
- Significant space given to proving the development exists and documenting its scale
- Effects and countermoves are briefly gestured at rather than developed
- The nut graf frames the development as the revelation
- Sources are primarily people experiencing or causing the development, not yet people reacting to it

**Signals of a mature story**:
- Development is stated briefly in the first paragraph and not proved out
- Lead focuses on an effect, consequence, or countermove — the development is assumed known
- Sources include institutional actors responding to the development (lawyers, regulators, advocates)
- The story has a "what's being done about it" structure
- Language of time: "now", "in response", "since the original reports"

---

## What each stage implies about gaps

**Juvenile story gaps** — expected and forgivable:
- Effects underdeveloped (the story is still establishing the development)
- Countermoves absent or minimal (may not have formed yet)
- Futures section thin (too early to project)
- Emotional responses of affected parties missing or brief

These gaps don't mean the reporter failed — they mean the story is early. The pipeline is: develop the effects, then the countermoves, then the institutional response.

**Juvenile story gaps** — damning:
- Scope not documented (only one example when the story claims something widespread)
- Reasons absent (why is this happening now? who benefits?)
- Key constituencies missing (the people most directly harmed don't appear)
- Central development established only through observers, not actors

**Mature story gaps** — expected:
- Scope section brief (the development is known; rehashing it is padding)
- History section brief or absent (same reason)

**Mature story gaps** — damning:
- Countermoves described but not shown in action
- Effects documented by category but not by specific case
- Institutional response absent when the development is old enough to have triggered one
- The story doesn't advance beyond what was in the original juvenile coverage

---

## The flip case

Sometimes a story presents as mature but is actually still juvenile — the reporter treats the development as known when it isn't, or the prior coverage was thin enough that the "given" isn't established.

Signs of this:
- The nut graf asserts something significant without providing the earlier coverage as context
- The "everyone knows" framing is doing work it hasn't earned
- Key details about the development's scope are missing and would matter to a new reader

When you find this, note it explicitly. The story has a credibility problem that affects how much weight to put on its chain extensions.
