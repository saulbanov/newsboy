# Extraction Guide

Read the story for its implicit cause-effect map. You are not summarizing — you are reverse-engineering the reporting decisions the journalist made and didn't make.

---

## What to find

Work through the Blundell categories against the story text:

**Central development**
What is the engine? The event or current of events that everything else in the story flows from. State it in one plain sentence. If the story buries or obscures it, note that — it's a structural signal.

**History**
Does the story place the development in historical context? Is it a clean break with the past, a continuation of an established pattern, or something that can only be understood against prior events? Historical context explaining *why this is happening now* is often the most important missing element in regulatory and environmental stories — prior violations, ownership chains, regulatory history that the reporter didn't pursue or couldn't get.
Mark as: documented, gestured at, or absent.

**Scope**
How does the story define the scale of the development? Look for:
- Quantitative evidence (numbers, percentages, counts)
- Geographic range (local, regional, national)
- Diversity (how many different ways the development manifests)
- Perspective (what other developments bear on this one)

Mark each dimension as: documented, implied, or absent.

**Reasons**
Does the story explain why this is happening now? Economic, political/legal, social, psychological? The psychological/personality reasons are most often absent. Note specifically which reasons are given and which are skipped.

**Impacts**
Who is being hurt or helped, and how? Note:
- Which impacts are shown in action vs. described by observers
- Whether emotional responses are present or only analytical opinions
- Which affected groups appear vs. which are conspicuously absent

**Countermoves**
Who is fighting back, adapting, or exploiting the situation? Note:
- Whether countermoves are shown in action or only discussed
- Whether the story distinguishes between people talking about fighting back and people actually doing it
- How much space countermoves get relative to the development and impacts

**Futures**
Does the story gesture at where this is heading? Formal projections? Informal opinion? Reporter's own inference?

For story stage (juvenile vs. mature), see `references/story-stage-assessment.md`.

## Signals for understated elements

These patterns indicate something is present but underweighted — worth flagging separately from outright absence:

**The brief mention**: A constituency or effect appears in one sentence then disappears. This often signals the reporter knew it was there but didn't have time or access to develop it. High-value gap.

**The observer substitution**: A person who has direct experience of the development is replaced by an expert who only has opinion about it. Signals the reporter couldn't get to the actor. The actor's story is the unreported one.

**The passive countermove**: "Advocates are calling for..." or "Critics say..." without any named person taking a named action. The actual countermove, if it exists, is unreported.

**The generalized effect**: "Many families are struggling" without a specific family shown struggling. The reporter established the category but didn't document it. The documentation is the follow story.

**The missing constituency**: A group logically affected by the development that doesn't appear at all. Not a gap the reporter acknowledged — a gap they didn't notice or couldn't access.

## Constituency audit

This is the single most productive gap-finding exercise. After reading the story, ask: who is touched by this development that doesn't appear?

Work outward from the central development in rings:
- Direct actors (people pressing buttons or getting ground up)
- Institutional actors (agencies, companies, governments with formal roles)
- Downstream affected parties (people who feel effects without being directly involved)
- Observers with standing (people whose expertise or role gives their opinion weight)
- Latent constituencies (groups who don't know yet they're affected)

The latent constituency is almost always absent from juvenile stories. It's often the richest follow-on story.

---

## What to record

For each category, record:
- What's present (with a brief note on how it's handled — documented, implied, observer-only, etc.)
- What's thin (present but underdeveloped)
- What's absent

This becomes the story map that feeds the projection pass.
