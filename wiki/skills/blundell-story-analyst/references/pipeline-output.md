# Pipeline Output

How to construct follow-on story theme statements from projected chain elements, and how to present the pipeline so it's usable.

---

## Building a follow-on theme statement

A follow-on theme statement follows the same Blundell skeleton as any theme statement:

**[Central development] → [most important consequence] → [reaction, if present]**

But it starts from the projected chain element, not from the original story's development.

**Example**:

Original story establishes: Oil operator illegally disposing of produced water in Johnson County. Landowners near disposal sites unaware.

Projected element (near-certain): Affected landowners will have observable property damage or health effects they haven't connected to the source.

Follow-on theme statement:
> Landowners near illegal produced water disposal sites in Johnson County are reporting unexplained well contamination and crop failures, and at least some are beginning to connect the damage to the operator — raising the prospect of civil liability the original enforcement action didn't address.

This is a publishable story with its own development (landowner harm), effect (property damage, health effects), and countermove (emerging litigation threat).

---

## What makes a strong pipeline entry

**Specificity**: Name the constituency, the geography, the institution. "Affected landowners in Johnson County" is stronger than "people near disposal sites."

**Actionability**: A good pipeline entry tells the reporter exactly what they'd need to confirm it. "Three calls to county extension agents would establish whether crop damage reports have increased" is more useful than "further reporting required."

**Stage clarity**: Label whether the follow-on story would be juvenile (this development is unreported) or mature (this effect is known but the response isn't). Shapes the reporting approach before the reporter starts.

**Source indication**: Where possible, note who would have the information. Named institutions, record types, or source categories. "Railroad Commission violation records for this operator" is a start. "County health department complaint logs" is a start. Don't invent sources — only note ones implied by the established chain.

---

## Ranking the pipeline

Return 2-3 strongest follow-on stories, not an exhaustive list. More than three is noise.

Rank by:

1. **Near-certain + specific constituency + short path to confirmation** — highest priority
2. **Probable + clear source availability** — worth pursuing
3. **Near-certain but requiring significant access** — high value, high cost

Don't include speculative elements in the pipeline unless there's a specific reason to flag them (e.g., if confirmed it would significantly change the story's implications).

---

## The pipeline as a competitive tool

When running this skill on a competitor's story, the pipeline has a secondary use: it tells you which follow-on stories they're likely working on vs. which ones they probably missed.

A reporter who just published a juvenile story is almost certainly developing the effects and countermoves. If their story has a named source who is "exploring legal options," assume they have the litigation story.

The gaps worth pursuing competitively are:
- Constituencies that don't appear in the story and aren't implied by its sourcing
- Effects that follow from the development but require a different reporting approach (data, documents, on-the-ground in a different geography)
- The mature version of a juvenile story — if they just established the development, the "what's being done about it" story is open

The gaps not worth pursuing:
- Straightforward depth on the same sources they used
- Effects they gestured at but didn't develop (they're probably coming back to these)
