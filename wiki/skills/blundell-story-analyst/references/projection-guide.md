# Projection Guide

Extend the extracted chain forward from where the story stops. The goal is not to speculate freely — it is to follow cause-and-effect logic from what is established, applying the same scrutiny Blundell recommends for story scoping.

---

## The projection method

Take each documented element in the story map and ask: what logically follows from this?

Work the chain in three ways:

**Forward from effects**: If this effect is real and ongoing, what does it cause next? Who else gets pulled in? What institutions are eventually forced to respond?

**Forward from countermoves**: If this countermove is underway, what are its likely outcomes? Who opposes it? What does success or failure look like?

**Constituency follow-through**: For each actor or affected group in the story map, ask what is happening to them that the story didn't follow. This is not a chain extension — it's a lateral move to a different perspective on the same chain. Often produces the richest gaps because reporters tend to follow one thread and drop the others.

---

## Confidence labeling

Every projected element must carry one of three labels. Be disciplined — mislabeling near-certain as speculative buries high-value leads; mislabeling speculative as near-certain wastes reporters' time.

**`near-certain`**
Apply when:
- The logical connection is tight and direct
- Enough time has passed for the effect to materialize
- There are no obvious external forces that would have disrupted the logic
- It would be surprising if this *hadn't* happened

Example: A facility has been illegally disposing of waste for three years. Near-certain that there are affected landowners downwind or downstream who don't know the source of their problem.

**`probable`**
Apply when:
- The logical connection is sound but requires a step or two
- External forces might have slowed or altered the effect
- The time frame is right but the effect may be partial rather than fully developed
- There's no evidence for it yet, but it's worth a check call

Example: Railroad Commission is receiving complaints about the same operator from multiple counties. Probable that there's a pattern in the violation records, but depends on how the RRC categorizes them.

**`speculative`**
Apply when:
- The connection requires multiple assumptions
- It's logical but early — may not have had time to materialize
- Depends heavily on factors the story doesn't establish
- Would require significant external evidence to confirm

Example: A contamination story might eventually produce litigation. Speculative until there's a lawyer in the picture.

---

## The three scrutiny tests

Before assigning `near-certain` or `probable`, run Blundell's three tests for remote chain elements:

**Time**: Has enough time passed for this effect to exist? A development reported six months ago has had more time to generate downstream effects than one reported last week.

**Distance**: How many steps is this from the central development? The further from the engine, the more external forces may have intervened. Flag if external factors could have disrupted the logic.

**Constituency**: Is there an interest group or institution that would be expected to react and hasn't? Absence of an expected countermove is itself information — either the countermove is unreported, hasn't materialized, or has been suppressed.

---

## What makes a strong chain extension

The most valuable projections are ones where:
- A near-certain effect involves a specific named constituency that didn't appear in the story
- The gap between what's established and what follows is small enough to close with a few calls or a records request
- The follow-on story is a different *type* than the original — the original was juvenile (establishing the development); the follow-on is mature (what's being done about it)

The least valuable projections are:
- Abstract systemic effects ("this will affect the economy")
- Effects that require significant time to materialize
- Effects that depend on political or institutional action that hasn't begun

---

## Distinguishing gaps from follow-on stories

Not every gap is a follow-on story. Some gaps are just thin reporting.

**Gap**: The story establishes that waste is being dumped but doesn't show a specific affected family. The gap is reportable but it's filling out the existing story, not a new one.

**Follow-on story**: The story establishes that waste is being dumped and regulators know. The follow-on story is: why haven't regulators acted, and who has been protecting the operator? That's a new development with its own cause-effect chain.

The test: does the unreported element extend the existing chain (same story, more depth) or start a new one (different central development, different effects)?

Both are valuable. Label them differently.
