# Distribution Prep

The story is done. This skill's job is to get it to the right people, in the right form, through the right channels.

## What this skill sees

The cleared draft. Nothing else.

## What this skill produces

### 1. Headline options
Three options, each a different approach:
- **Declarative** — states what happened
- **Question** — surfaces the unknown the story answers
- **Impact-first** — leads with who is affected and how

For each: a note on SEO considerations and platform fit. The reporter and editor choose.

### 2. Social copy
Platform-specific, not one-size copy pasted across channels:

- **X/Twitter** — one punchy line + link. 240 characters or under. Hook first.
- **Facebook/LinkedIn** — 2-3 sentences. Context before the hook. Audience may not know the beat.
- **Instagram caption** (if multimedia assets exist) — visual-first framing, story as context.
- **Newsletter blurb** — 3-4 sentences. Assumes a reader who trusts the outlet. Focuses on why this matters now.

Each piece of social copy must be accurate to the story. No overstatement to chase clicks.

### 3. Stakeholder contact list
Who needs to know about this story before or at publication, and in what form:

- **Affected parties** — people or organizations whose situation the story documents. Do they get advance notice? In what form?
- **Amplifiers** — journalists, advocates, institutions, or accounts likely to share if contacted directly. What framing works for each?
- **Sources who contributed** — do they get a heads-up? A copy? A thank-you?
- **Potential critics** — anyone likely to push back. Flag for the reporter and editor to decide how to handle.

The list does not make decisions — it surfaces who exists and what their relationship to the story is. The journalist decides who gets contacted, when, and how.

### 4. Multimedia artifact briefs
Based on the editorial review's multimedia flags, produce a brief for each flagged artifact:

- **What it is** — chart, map, graphic, photo, video, data download
- **What it needs to show** — the specific claim or moment it supports
- **What data or material is required** — source, dataset, location, subject
- **Priority** — essential (story is weaker without it) / adds value / nice to have

These are briefs, not finished products. They go to whoever produces multimedia assets.

### 5. Distribution sequence
Suggested order of publication actions:
1. Pre-publication stakeholder outreach (if any)
2. Publication
3. Social push sequence (which platform first, timing)
4. Follow-up amplifier outreach

Timing suggestions based on story type — breaking news, investigation, feature — with rationale.

## Non-negotiables

- Social copy must be accurate. No overstatement. If a headline would embarrass the reporter when read against the story, flag it.
- Stakeholder list is a map, not a decision. The journalist decides who gets contacted.
- Multimedia briefs are only produced for artifacts flagged during editorial review. Do not invent new artifact ideas here — that was editorial review's job.
- Distribution sequence is a suggestion, not an instruction. The audience team and editor have final say.
