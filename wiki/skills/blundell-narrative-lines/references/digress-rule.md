# Digress Often, But Don't Digress for Long

The most important organizational principle for time line stories, and a useful discipline for all three lines.

---

## What a digression is

A digression is any story element that doesn't have salient action in it. Observations, explanations, descriptions, points of instruction, analysis — all digressions. The best quotes in a story are digressions too.

In brief: most of a typical story's content consists of digressions. The only elements that are not digressions are those in which something is actually happening — something that pushes the story forward.

---

## Why the rule matters

If a story is a river dotted by reservoirs, the still waters are the passages of digression. Floating lazily on them, the reader can forget the river exists and fall asleep. The writer's job is to hustle the reader over the lakes as fast as possible and get back into white water.

Digressions are necessary. They provide variety, information, context, and texture. Without them, a story is just a sequence of events — exciting but thin. The discipline is not to eliminate digressions but to move through them briskly.

---

## The cowboy story as illustration

Blundell's cowboy trail ride section runs through 16 paragraphs. Here is what the action/digression pattern looks like:

- P13: No action. Observation by the cowboss.
- P14: No action. Observation by the reporter.
- P15-16: Mostly action, ending in explanation.
- P17: Explanation by the cowboss.
- P18: Action followed by observation.
- P19: No action. Quotes.
- P20: Action. Weather front moves in, launching observations about hazards.
- P21: All action. Men driving cattle.
- P22-23: No action. Digressive passage on handling cattle.
- P24: Action. Riders reach the plain, return to the bunkhouse, have lunch.
- P25-27: Digression on cowboy codes and personnel practices.
- P28: Action. End of the trail ride section.

The longest continuous digression is three paragraphs (P25-27). Most digressions are one to one and a half paragraphs before something happens again. The action stitches the story together; the digressions provide everything else.

Note how digressions are triggered: the weather front suggests lightning hazards. The horses commuting in a van triggers digressions on modern ranching. The tortuous climb up Mingus suggests observations about the importance of high country. Each digression is prompted by action — it grows out of what just happened and returns to action.

---

## Practical application

Every time you start a passage of digression, set a mental timer. Get out fast. This forces you to:
- Write more economically
- Slash repetitive material
- Select only the best elements within each digression
- Pull things together instead of letting them sprawl

The discipline is especially important in time line stories, where long digressions make the reader lose the thread of the timeline. But it applies to all three narrative lines. Block progression stories can sprawl within sections. Theme line stories can hammer a facet for too long before returning to action.

The test: could you cut the last paragraph of this digression without losing anything essential? If yes, cut it.
