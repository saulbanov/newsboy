# Block Progression Section Order

The theme statement determines the order of sections. Here is the method.

---

## Step 1: Read the theme statement for emphasis

The section that receives the most stress in your theme statement is almost always the first section you write in the body (after the nut graf establishes the main theme).

Examples:

**Theme statement**: "An acute shortage of physicians is developing, and the quality and availability of medical care is being affected as a result."
→ The shortage itself is news. Scope leads. Effects are secondary.

**Theme statement**: "The deterioration of medical care from the doctor shortage is generating malpractice judgments pushing insurance rates through the roof, and many physicians are going without coverage or forming self-insurance pools."
→ The development is given. The forward elements dominate. Impacts leads, with Countermoves close behind.

**Theme statement**: "Prime farmland is being lost at a worrisome rate, and states and counties are scrambling to pass measures to keep their countrysides green."
→ Scope and Countermoves both stressed. Scope leads (establishing the problem), Countermoves follows.

---

## Step 2: Plan the first two sections deliberately

Think hard about what your first detailed section should be and why. The second section is usually the one most closely connected to the first — what the first section points toward naturally.

After that: stop planning. Let what you've written suggest what comes next. The writing will point toward its own next move. Trying to preplan every section before you write produces tortured, empty transitional passages that force the story in a direction it doesn't want to follow.

---

## Step 3: Follow the road signs

As you finish each section, ask: what has the last paragraph pointed toward? The natural next section is usually signaled by the material itself. This is Blundell's principle: "Let what you have already written suggest what you write next."

This doesn't mean jumping out of a section before it's done. Stay within each section until it's complete. But use the principle to move between sections when one ends and the next needs to begin.

---

## Section order patterns by story type

**Juvenile story** (development is news):
Scope → Reasons → Impacts → Countermoves → Futures
(Prove the development, explain why it's happening, show its effects, show the response)

**Mature story** (development is given):
Impacts → Countermoves → [Scope briefly] → Futures
(Lead with the unreported news, show the response, acknowledge the known background)

**Logic-defying development** (development is newsy but reasons are the real story):
Reasons → Scope → Impacts → Countermoves
(Explain why first, then prove it, then show effects)

These are patterns, not rules. A story may depart from any of them if the material points another way.

---

## History: never a block

History is the exception to the keep-related-material-together principle. Too much history in one lump draws the reader away from immediate events that are usually the story's main concern.

Sprinkle historical material wherever it provides contrast, authenticity, or context. Use it to illuminate a present moment — not to explain the past for its own sake. A cowboy's current lunch gains interest when we're told what his counterpart ate a century ago. Use history as seasoning, not a course.
