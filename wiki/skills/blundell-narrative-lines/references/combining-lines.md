# Combining Narrative Lines

Most real stories use more than one line. Name the primary line; note where others are embedded.

---

## The relief pitcher story

Primary line: Theme line — two facets (the pitcher's mounting futility, the differences between minor and major league ball) hammered throughout.

Embedded: A time line when the writer follows the team for a week. The themes continue through the time line, but the structure changes — for that portion, the reader is moving through time, not through argument.

---

## The development story with a miniprofile

Primary line: Block progression — sections organized by scope, reasons, impacts, countermoves.

Embedded: A brief theme line when the story needs to characterize a key personality. Two or three paragraphs treating one actor's qualities — essentially a miniprofile inserted into the development story. Functions like a focused digression from the block structure, then returns to it.

Also possible: A brief time line covering a limited but significant chain of events — the three days during which a regulatory decision was made, for instance.

---

## The profile with a time line

Primary line: Theme line — two facets of the subject established in the theme statement, hammered throughout.

Embedded: A time line section — a day, a process, a journey — that generates action for the digressions to attach to. The time line serves the theme line; it's a vehicle for revealing the facets, not a story about the passage of time itself.

---

## How to identify combinations in your own story

Ask: are there any portions of the story where:
- Chronology imposes itself and would be awkward to violate? → time line embedded
- You need to characterize a person in depth separate from the main structure? → theme line embedded
- A sequence of events needs to be told as a chain rather than as separate blocks? → time line embedded

Name the embedded line, note roughly where it appears, and confirm that returning to the primary line after the embedded section feels natural. If it doesn't, the embedded section may be fighting the primary structure and should be reconsidered.

---

## What doesn't work

Switching between all three lines in rapid succession without clear purpose produces the "jumbled, confusing" stories Blundell describes — the reader never knows what structure they're in or why it changed. Combinations work when each line serves a distinct purpose within the story. They fail when used to avoid the discipline of committing to one primary structure.
