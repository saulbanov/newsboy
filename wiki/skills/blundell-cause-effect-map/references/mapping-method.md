# Mapping Method

Step-by-step process for building the chain and drawing the fence.

---

## Step 1: Name the central development

State the thing that is happening in one plain verb sentence. No adjectives, no context, no framing. Just the event.

If you can't do this in one sentence, the idea isn't ready to map. It's still a topic, not a development. Push until you have a sentence with a subject, a verb, and a consequence implied.

Good: "Railroad Commission inspectors are systematically understating violations at produced water disposal sites."
Not ready: "There are problems with how Texas regulates oilfield waste."

---

## Step 2: Generate the chain

Treat the central development as a cause. Ask: what does this cause?

List every logical effect you can generate. Then treat each effect as a cause in turn. Ask again: what does this cause?

Rules for this step:
- Only action and reaction. No explanations, descriptions, or context.
- Give imagination free rein. Don't filter yet. Wild logical extensions are fine at this stage.
- Branch when the logic branches — one effect may produce multiple downstream effects.
- Keep going until the chain reaches effects so remote or speculative that the logic clearly breaks down.

---

## Step 3: Name the constituencies

After generating the chain, audit it for missing constituencies. For each link in the chain, ask: who is touched by this that doesn't appear?

Work outward in rings from each link:
- Who is directly affected (actors, not observers)?
- Which institutions have formal roles or responsibilities?
- Who is downstream — affected without being directly involved?
- Who is latent — affected but doesn't know it yet?

Add constituencies to the map where they belong in the chain. They often reveal effects that weren't visible.

---

## Step 4: Apply scrutiny tests

For each element more than one step from the central development, run the three scrutiny tests (see `references/scrutiny-tests.md`): time, distance, external disruptors. Label each element near-certain, probable, or speculative.

Speculative elements don't disappear from the map — they get flagged. They may become near-certain as the story matures.

---

## Step 5: Draw the fence

Look at the full map. Identify the portion that is:
- Most important (the effects that matter most to the most people)
- Most reportable (accessible given this reporter's sources, geography, and timeline)
- Most coherent (a connected chain, not scattered elements from different branches)

The fence is the intersection of those three criteria.

State the fence explicitly: "This story covers [development] and its effects on [constituency A] and [constituency B], through [timeframe]. It does not cover [major element outside fence] — that is a separate story."

Everything outside the fence is either a future story or another reporter's story. Note the most promising outside-fence elements so they don't get lost.

---

## Step 6: Hand off to theme statement

The fenced portion of the map is the raw material for the theme statement. The development at the center of the fence becomes the first clause. The most important effect becomes the second clause. The most significant countermove, if it exists, becomes the third.

Run `blundell-theme-statement` in interactive mode using the fenced map as the starting point.
