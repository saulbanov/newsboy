# Scrutiny Tests for Remote Chain Elements

The further an element is from the central development, the less certain it is to exist. Before chasing a remote element, apply three tests. Fail any one and treat the element as speculative until there's early evidence.

---

## Test 1: Time

Has enough time passed for this effect to have materialized?

A development that started six months ago has had time to generate second-order effects. A development announced last week has not. Effects that are logically certain given enough time may simply not exist yet because the story is too young.

Ask: is this a story about what is happening, or about what will happen? The latter is a futures section, not a reporting priority now.

---

## Test 2: Distance

How many logical steps is this element from the central development?

Each step introduces the possibility that external forces have intruded and disrupted the logic. A one-step effect (development → direct consequence) is usually reliable. A three-step effect may be perfectly logical but may have been derailed by factors the chain doesn't account for.

The classic example: a doctor shortage logically leads to malpractice claims, which logically leads to higher insurance rates. But the insurance rate step may not have materialized because insurers are competing for market share and absorbing losses rather than raising premiums. The logic is intact; external forces broke the chain.

Before chasing a remote element, ask: what external forces could have disrupted this link? If you can name plausible disruptors, the element needs early evidence before it earns a reporting trip.

---

## Test 3: Constituencies

Have all the interest groups and institutions touched by the development been named?

The obvious constituencies are never all of them. Work outward in rings: who is directly involved, who is institutionally involved, who is downstream, who is latent (affected but doesn't know it yet)?

This test also runs in reverse: if a constituency that should exist doesn't appear in the chain, ask why. Is it because the effect hasn't reached them yet, or because the development isn't as significant as assumed?

---

## Confidence labels

After running all three tests, assign one of three labels to each remote element:

**Near-certain**: Tight logical connection, sufficient time, no obvious disruptors, specific constituency named. Would be surprising if this hadn't happened. High reporting priority.

**Probable**: Sound logic, right time frame, but one or two assumptions required. Worth a check call before committing to a reporting trip.

**Speculative**: Multiple steps, early timing, or known disruptors. Flag it and set it aside until there's early evidence it exists.

---

## The preconception trap

The map is a hypothesis. A reporter who holds too tightly to their map — straining again and again to find proof where little exists, ignoring contrary evidence — becomes a propagandist for their own logic, not a journalist. 

Use the map to focus reporting, not to predetermine findings. When the reporting contradicts the map, the reporting wins.
