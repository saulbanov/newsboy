# Failure Modes — Too Small and Too Big

Before mapping, diagnose which failure mode is more likely. Most reporters default to one or the other habitually.

---

## Too small

**What it looks like**: The development is limited and its extent can be defined in a few sentences. The reasons behind it are obvious. The effects are apparent. The reporter sits down to write and finds the story resolves into two or three pages of honest copy — not enough for an important piece.

**What happens next**: The reporter lards the piece with repetitive quotes, irrelevant facts, and numbers. The story feels padded because it is padded.

**Diagnostic signals**:
- The central development has only one or two obvious effects
- The constituencies touched are few and immediately apparent
- The reasons behind the development are self-evident
- There is no countermove to speak of
- The reporter has covered similar ground before and finds it familiar

**The fix**: Extend the chain. Ask what the effects of the effects are. Ask who else is touched that isn't obvious. Ask whether the development is part of a larger pattern. The story may be too small as framed but right-sized if reframed at a higher level of the chain.

---

## Too big

**What it looks like**: The reporter tries to embrace the circus fat lady — a development so vast that covering it adequately in one story is impossible. The result is a piece impossibly long, or superficial, the reporter skipping frantically from point to point without dwelling on any long enough to illuminate and convince. The pace is frenzied; the reader is wearied.

**What happens next**: A limited tale well told has more impact than a sweeping story that can't be adequately illustrated. The too-big story usually ends up proving nothing because it tries to prove everything.

**Diagnostic signals**:
- The cause-and-effect map has more than a dozen distinct elements
- Multiple major constituencies each deserve their own story
- The reporter would need to be in several places at once to report it fully
- Every time the reporter thinks they're done, they realize they've missed another angle
- The story has been "in progress" for a long time without a clear shape

**The fix**: Draw the fence. Choose one chain of effects — the most important and most reportable — and leave the rest for other stories or other reporters. A good fence removes the guilt of not covering everything because it acknowledges the choice explicitly.

---

## The fence as the solution to both

The cause-and-effect map solves both failure modes by the same mechanism: it makes the full shape of the territory visible, so the reporter can see clearly what portion they're choosing and why.

For the too-small story: the map often reveals that the territory is larger than it looked. The development connects to effects the reporter hadn't considered, and the story grows to fit.

For the too-big story: the map makes visible just how much territory exists — and makes it possible to choose a manageable section of it deliberately rather than discovering the problem at the writing stage.
