# Accountability Tests — Audit Mode

Use this when the reporter has an existing pitch or pitch notes and wants it stress-tested before presenting to an editor or funder.

Run each test in sequence. Return findings as: **test name → assessment → specific fix**.

---

## Test 1: Harm concreteness

**The question**: Is the harm specific enough that a reader could visualize it happening to a real person?

**Pass**: "Children at Pleasant View Elementary were exposed to PFAS at 14x the EPA safety threshold for four years while the district knew and said nothing."

**Fail**: "Communities near oilfield waste sites face health risks."

**Common fixes**:
- Add numbers where you have them (concentrations, affected population count, dollar amounts)
- Name the community or institution instead of using a category
- Add the timeline ("for four years," "since 2019")
- Move from potential harm to documented harm if the reporting supports it

---

## Test 2: Responsibility clarity

**The question**: Is there a specific decision-maker who made a choice that caused or perpetuated the harm?

**Pass**: "The Texas Railroad Commission received test results showing contamination in 2021 and did not notify affected residents or require remediation."

**Fail**: "Regulatory failure allowed contamination to continue."

**Common fixes**:
- Name the institution or agency, not just the category
- Name the specific action or inaction: what was done or not done?
- Add the decision point: when did they know, and what did they do or not do?
- If individual culpability is provable, name individuals — but only if the evidence supports it

---

## Test 3: Novelty

**The question**: What specifically hasn't been reported? What would a reader learn from this story that they can't get from existing coverage?

**Pass**: "The contamination was reported two years ago, but no story has connected it to the specific waste disposal contractor with 14 prior violations in three states, or to the families now seeking legal action."

**Fail**: "This is an important ongoing issue that deserves more coverage."

**Common fixes**:
- Do a fast literature review and list the three most recent stories on this topic
- Identify the specific gap: geography, timeframe, affected population, causal link, or institutional connection not yet reported
- If there is no gap, be honest — the pitch may need to be repositioned as advancing existing coverage rather than breaking new ground

---

## Test 4: Sourcing adequacy

**The question**: Are the key claims in the pitch supported by documents, on-record sources, or data — or by inference and background conversations?

Walk through each major claim in the pitch and classify:
- **Document-backed**: Government records, court filings, test results, contracts
- **Source-backed**: Named, on-record person who will attest to this
- **Data-backed**: Quantitative dataset that supports the claim
- **Thin**: Background source, anonymous tip, logical inference, or "widely known"

A pitch with major claims in the "thin" category is a story hypothesis, not a story. That's not a death sentence — most pitches start there — but it needs to be honest.

---

## Test 5: Doability

**The question**: Can the reporter realistically get what's needed to publish the minimum story?

Assess:
- **Timeline**: Is the minimum story achievable in the time available?
- **Access**: Does the reporter have credible paths to the key sources and documents?
- **Legal exposure**: Are there foreseeable legal obstacles that could kill or delay publication?
- **Competition**: Is another outlet known to be working on this?

A story can pass all four previous tests and still fail on doability. That's important information for an editor.
