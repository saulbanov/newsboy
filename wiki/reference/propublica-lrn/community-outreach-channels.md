# Community Outreach Channels

## By community type

### Rural communities
- **Facebook groups**: Highest-reach channel for rural communities. Search "[county name] community", "[town name] neighbors", "[county name] farmers/ranchers". Request to join and introduce yourself before posting.
- **Local newspapers**: Even small weeklies have loyal readerships and may be willing to run a callout if you share credit.
- **Churches and faith communities**: In many rural areas, pastors are trusted information nodes. A pastoral endorsement of a callout dramatically increases response.
- **Co-ops and farm bureaus**: Agricultural communities have institutional networks. Extension offices often have email lists.
- **Feed stores, grain elevators, local diners**: Physical flyers still work in communities where people gather in person.
- **County commissioners and local officials**: Mixed — some will actively help, others will alert the subject of your investigation.

### Working-class and low-income urban communities
- **Nextdoor**: Highly localized. Useful for neighborhood-specific stories.
- **WhatsApp groups**: Often organized by block, building, or workplace. Harder to access without an intermediary.
- **Community organizations**: Tenant unions, food banks, community health centers. These organizations have existing trust with residents and can vouch for you.
- **Legal aid organizations**: Clients of legal aid are often exactly the people affected by the stories you're covering. Legal aid staff can make referrals (with client consent).
- **Churches**: Especially relevant for stories involving immigrant communities or communities of color.
- **Union halls**: For workplace safety, labor, or wage theft stories.

### Immigrant communities
- **Spanish-language Facebook groups**: Separate from English-language groups; often very active.
- **WhatsApp networks**: The dominant communication tool in many immigrant communities. Requires a trusted intermediary to get in.
- **Ethnic media**: Spanish-language radio and TV, Vietnamese-language newspapers, etc. — these outlets have existing trust and distribution. Partner with them rather than competing.
- **Cultural associations and consulates**: For stories involving specific nationalities.
- **Churches**: Catholic churches in particular often serve as community hubs for Latin American immigrant communities.
- **Worker centers**: For labor and workplace stories involving undocumented workers.
- **Language and translation**: Any callout aimed at non-English-speaking communities must be in their language. Machine translation is a starting point, not a finish.

### Professional communities (nurses, teachers, first responders, etc.)
- **LinkedIn**: Effective for professionals. Direct outreach to individuals by job title and geography.
- **Professional associations**: State nursing associations, teachers unions, firefighter associations. They have email lists and may distribute callouts to members.
- **Trade publications and newsletters**: Often read closely by professionals. A mention in a trade pub is worth many general-audience posts.
- **Reddit**: Professional subreddits (r/nursing, r/teachers, etc.) have active communities willing to share workplace experiences.
- **Former employee networks**: LinkedIn again. "Former employees of [company]" searches are useful.

### Online-first communities (specific platforms, interest groups)
- **Facebook groups** by topic: "Texas environmental concerns", "PFAS contamination victims"
- **Reddit**: r/[city], r/[topic]
- **Twitter/X**: Useful for reaching journalists, advocates, and politically engaged people; less useful for reaching affected community members directly
- **TikTok and Instagram**: Younger and working-class audiences; hard to use for document callouts but good for awareness-building that funnels to a form

## Trusted intermediary strategy

The highest-yield engagement approach for hard-to-reach communities is finding a trusted intermediary — someone already trusted by the community who can vouch for you and share your callout.

Characteristics of a good intermediary:
- Known and trusted by the target community (not just an institutional contact)
- Understands and endorses the journalism (not just willing to do you a favor)
- Has a direct communication channel to community members (listserv, WhatsApp group, pulpit)

Caution: Intermediaries can also shape who responds. An advocate intermediary will send people who support the advocate's position. A church intermediary may not reach people who don't attend. Build in a direct channel alongside the intermediary channel.

## Platform-specific callout tips

**Facebook groups**:
- Post as a person, not an organization, if allowed
- Introduce yourself and explain what you're doing before posting the callout
- Respond to every comment in the first few hours — engagement drives reach
- Pin important follow-up info

**Jotform / Google Forms**:
- Keep to 5 questions or fewer for an initial callout
- Always include a freeform text box: "Is there anything else you want us to know?"
- Include a phone number field even if your primary collection is text — some people would rather call
- Test the form on mobile before publishing; most respondents will use a phone

**Dedicated email address**:
- Create a purpose-specific address (wastestory@[outlet].com) rather than using a personal address
- Set up an auto-response that thanks them and tells them what happens next
- Check it daily once the callout is live
