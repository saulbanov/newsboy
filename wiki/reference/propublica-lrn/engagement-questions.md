# Engagement Questions — Design Mode

Use when building an engagement strategy from scratch. Work through these in order. Don't run through all questions mechanically — skip sections where the reporter has clear answers.

---

## Stage 1: What do you need from people?

Before designing anything, establish what engagement is supposed to produce.

Ask: "What would respondents give you that would materially improve this story?"

Valid answers (specific and actionable):
- Documents they have that you don't: test results, permits, correspondence, photos
- Data points that become a dataset: dates, locations, dosages, financial losses
- Testimony that establishes a pattern: "this happened to me too"
- Leads to sources you can't find through institutional channels

Invalid answers (too vague):
- "Their stories" — stories aren't a reporting category
- "Their perspective" — perspective doesn't appear in accountability journalism without specifics
- "Awareness" — awareness is a PR goal, not a journalism goal

If the reporter can't say specifically what they need, the engagement plan can't be designed. Press until they can name it.

---

## Stage 2: Who specifically do you want to respond?

Ask: "Describe the person you most want to hear from — not a demographic category, but a specific type of person with a specific experience."

The more specific the target, the better the outreach design. "People affected by oilfield waste" is too broad. "Parents of children who attended Pleasant View Elementary between 2015 and 2021 who noticed health problems" is specific enough to design outreach around.

Then ask: "Why would they participate? What do they get out of it?"

Common motivators:
- Desire for accountability: "Someone finally doing something about this"
- Relief at being believed: "I've been trying to tell people for years"
- Practical benefit: legal referrals, health resources, community connection
- Civic duty for less directly affected people

Common barriers:
- Fear of retaliation (employer, landlord, immigration status)
- Distrust of press
- Language barriers
- Time, access to technology
- Belief that nothing will change anyway

Design the outreach to address the dominant barrier for this specific community.

---

## Stage 3: Where do you find them?

Ask: "If you wanted to reach 100 of the right people, where would they be — online and offline?"

Work through channels systematically. See `references/community-outreach-channels.md` for channel guide by community type.

Key principle: **go to where the community already is** rather than expecting them to come to you. A callout posted only on the newsroom website reaches readers, not necessarily affected people.

Ask about:
- Social media groups (Facebook groups, Nextdoor, WhatsApp networks)
- Institutions (churches, schools, clinics, union halls, community organizations)
- Networks (legal aid organizations, advocacy groups, professional associations)
- Trusted intermediaries (community leaders, local pastors, organizers who can vouch for you)

---

## Stage 4: What's the ask, exactly?

Ask: "If you had 30 seconds with the right person and could only ask them one thing, what would you ask?"

That is your callout's primary ask. Everything else is secondary.

The ask must be:
- Answerable in the time a busy person has (3 minutes maximum for a form)
- Specific enough that you know what to do with the answer
- Low-stakes enough that people will do it without being sure you're trustworthy

If you need more than one thing, design a two-stage process: easy first ask to identify respondents, harder ask (documents, interview) for the subset who engage.

---

## Stage 5: Collection and verification

Ask: "How will people submit, and how will you know what they send is credible?"

Collection options (easiest to hardest for the respondent):
- Text a number
- Fill out a simple form (Jotform, Google Form, DocumentCloud)
- Email a dedicated address
- Upload a document
- Schedule a call

Verification approaches:
- Cross-reference responses against public records
- Look for corroborating patterns across responses (same employer, same location, same timeline)
- Follow up with the most promising respondents for on-record interviews
- Use documents as leads to public records, not as standalone evidence

---

## Stage 6: Publication plan and reporting back

Ask: "When you publish, what will you say about how you used the community's input? And how will you let respondents know what happened?"

A credible engagement process includes:
- Transparency in the story about how you gathered community data
- A published note to respondents after the story runs
- A way for people who responded to find out if their information was used

If the reporter has no plan to report back, flag this as a trust problem — especially if they want to engage the same community in the future.
