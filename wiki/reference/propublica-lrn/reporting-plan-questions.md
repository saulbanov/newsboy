# Reporting Plan Questions — Build Mode

Use when a reporter has a pitch and needs a detailed execution plan. Work through these in order, one section at a time. Let their answers shape the depth of each section — don't run through all questions mechanically if the reporter has already answered them.

---

## Stage 1: The central question

Ask: "What is the specific question your reporting will answer?"

The central question is not the story's theme statement (that describes what the story is about). It's the operative unknown: the thing that, once answered, makes the story publishable.

**Test**: Can you answer it yes/no or with a specific fact? If not, it's still a topic.

- Weak: "How bad is the oilfield waste problem in Texas?"
- Strong: "Did [company] dispose of produced water in unlined pits in violation of its 2019 permit, and did the Railroad Commission know?"

If the reporter can't formulate a central question, back up and run `blundell-theme-statement` or `propublica-pitch` first.

---

## Stage 2: What you already know

Ask: "What have you already documented — documents, sources, data in hand?"

Sort everything into:
- **On-record statements**: quotes or attributed facts from named sources
- **Documents**: government records, permits, court filings, test results you've seen
- **Data**: datasets or quantitative evidence
- **Background**: things you know but can't yet attribute

The background category is the most dangerous. Treat it as a reporting gap, not a known fact.

---

## Stage 3: What you need to find out

Ask: "What are the most important things you still don't know?"

Rank by consequence:
1. Things that, if unknown, kill the story
2. Things that, if unknown, weaken the story significantly
3. Things that would strengthen the story but aren't required

The top two or three items in category 1 are your critical path.

---

## Stage 4: Source strategy

Ask: "Who are the people you need to talk to, and how are you going to get to them?"

For each major source type, establish:
- **Name or title** (even hypothetical: "former permit inspector, name unknown")
- **Access path** (LinkedIn, court records, referral from existing source, cold call)
- **Likely posture** (cooperative, cautious, hostile — and why)
- **What you need from them** (on-record quote, document, lead to another source)

See `references/source-strategy.md` for handling hostile and reluctant sources.

---

## Stage 5: Document targets

Ask: "What documents would prove the key claims in this story, and how will you get them?"

For each key claim, identify:
- What document would prove it (permit record, inspection report, email, contract, court filing)
- Where that document lives (agency, court, company)
- Whether it's public, FOIA-able, or only obtainable through a source
- If FOIA: which office, which exemptions are likely, what's the fallback if denied

A claim that can't be documented on the record by a source or document is not a publishable claim — it's a reporting goal.

---

## Stage 6: Challenges and countermeasures

Ask: "What could specifically go wrong, and what's your plan if it does?"

For each challenge, require a specific response:

| Challenge | Response |
|---|---|
| Key source won't go on record | Line up second source; find corroborating document |
| FOIA denied | Appeal; find agency insider; seek document through litigation records |
| Company stonewalls comment | Send detailed written questions by deadline; document non-response |
| Competing outlet on same story | Identify what's exclusive to this reporter; accelerate timeline |
| Legal threat | Consult news counsel before publication; document process |

---

## Stage 7: Past coverage assessment

Ask: "What's already been reported on this topic, and what specifically hasn't?"

The reporter should be able to name the three most recent substantial stories on this topic and explain in one sentence what gap each one left. If they can't, they haven't done enough background reading.

Common gap types:
- Geographic (reported nationally but not locally)
- Temporal (reported on the cause but not the current effects)
- Institutional (reported on one company/agency but not the one you're targeting)
- Legal (documents unsealed since last coverage)
- Human (the affected people haven't been interviewed)
