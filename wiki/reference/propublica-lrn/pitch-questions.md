# Pitch Questions — Build Mode

Use this when building a pitch from scratch. Ask one question at a time and let the reporter's answer drive what to probe next. Don't run through all questions mechanically — skip or combine sections where the reporter has already answered them.

---

## Know what you're watching for

These are the failure modes that kill pitches before they get assigned:

- **Harm without agency**: "People are suffering" with no identified decision-maker. Push until there's a person or institution who made a choice that caused the harm.
- **Novelty problem**: This story has already been reported. If you can find three pieces on the same topic in 30 seconds, so can the editor. Push the reporter to articulate specifically what hasn't been told.
- **Scope creep in the maximum**: Reporters routinely oversell. "This could change policy nationally" usually means "I hope someone notices." Ask them to name a specific mechanism for impact.
- **Thin minimum story**: The minimum story must be publishable on its own, not "we'll at least show there's a problem." Push for a concrete floor.
- **Sources too thin for the claims**: Check whether key claims rest on documents, named sources, or data — or on inference and background conversations. Flag each.

---

## Stage 1: The story in one sentence

Ask: "Describe what this story is about in one or two sentences — the thing that is happening or has happened."

If they give you a topic ("water contamination in rural Texas"), push for a development ("State regulators have known for three years that oilfield waste is leaching into drinking water in three counties and have not notified residents").

The test: does it have an actor, an action, and a consequence?

---

## Stage 2: The accountability frame

Ask: "What is the harm, and who is responsible for it?"

Probe for:
- **Who is harmed**: Specific people or communities, not abstractions.
- **How they are harmed**: Physical, financial, legal — be concrete.
- **Who is responsible**: Not "the system" — a named institution or class of decision-makers.
- **What decision caused the harm**: What was done, or not done, that a reasonable actor would have done differently?

If the reporter can't answer the last question, the accountability frame is thin. That doesn't kill the story, but it does change what kind of story it is.

---

## Stage 3: Why now

Ask: "Why does this story deserve to run next month and not two years ago or two years from now?"

Valid "why now" hooks:
- A triggering event (new data released, policy change, legal filing, death or incident)
- An anniversary or timeline event
- An upcoming decision point (vote, permit renewal, budget deadline)
- Exclusive access that creates a narrow window

If there is no "why now," the story may be real but not urgent. That's a problem for pitching. Help them find or manufacture urgency.

---

## Stage 4: What you know vs. what you need

Ask: "What do you already have — documents, sources, data — and what is the most important thing you don't yet have?"

Sort answers into three categories:
- **Solid**: Documents, on-record sources, data you've seen
- **Promising but unverified**: Tips, background sources, circumstantial evidence
- **Gaps**: Things the story needs but you don't have yet

The gap list is the core of the reporting plan. It tells the editor whether the story is doable.

---

## Stage 5: Characters

Ask: "Who are the key people in this story — the wrongdoers, the affected, the witnesses, the experts?"

Push for:
- Named individuals where possible, not just "a former employee"
- Affected community members who can speak on the record
- An antagonist who can be given right of response
- Expert voices who add credibility without replacing lived experience

---

## Stage 6: Challenges

Ask: "What could go wrong in the reporting?"

Probe for specifics:
- Documents that may be sealed, redacted, or hard to obtain
- Sources who may refuse to speak on record
- Legal obstacles (SLAPP threats, NDAs, pending litigation)
- Access problems (gated communities, private land, institutional stonewalling)
- Competing outlets who may be on the same story

Generic challenges ("it might take a long time") don't help. Specific obstacles ("the company has denied three FOIA requests from other reporters") do.

---

## Stage 7: Minimum and maximum story

Ask: "If the hardest parts don't come together — the key source won't talk, the documents stay sealed — what can you still publish? And if everything comes together, what's the best possible version?"

See `references/minimum-maximum.md` for calibration guidance.

---

## Stage 8: Collaboration and presentation

Ask: "Who else should be working on this, and how would you want it displayed?"

These are not afterthoughts for investigative work. A named partner (another outlet, a university researcher, a legal clinic) strengthens the pitch. A strong visual or data element signals you've thought beyond the text.
