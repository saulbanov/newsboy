# Gap Audit — Mid-Investigation Diagnostic

Use when a reporter is already reporting and wants to check whether their approach is on track or identify what's missing.

Run each section and return a one-paragraph assessment per active area, followed by a prioritized gap list (dealbreakers first).

---

## Audit section 1: Central question drift

The most common mid-investigation problem: the reporter has started chasing a different story than they pitched, often without realizing it.

Ask: "Read me your original central question. Now tell me what you've been reporting on for the last two weeks."

If the answers don't match, this is a fork decision, not a reporting gap:
- **Return to original question**: What's missing that would let you answer it?
- **Pivot to new story**: Has the new direction been pitched and approved? Does it still meet the accountability test?

Don't diagnose reporting gaps against an original question if the reporter has already moved off it — diagnose against wherever they actually are.

---

## Audit section 2: Sourcing balance

Check the reporter's source list against three dimensions:

**Tier**: Are they overweighted in one type?
- Too many experts/advocates, not enough affected people
- Too many named sources willing to talk, not enough hostile sources or non-respondents documented
- All sources from the same institutional context (e.g. all government, or all activists)

**Access**: Are key sources still untried?
- Has the reporter contacted the primary wrongdoer and documented their response or non-response?
- Has the reporter found at least one affected person who will speak on the record?
- Are there obvious sources — former employees, whistleblowers, litigants — who haven't been contacted?

**Posture**: Are the hostile sources being avoided?
- A reporting plan with only cooperative sources and no hostile sources is incomplete. The other side's response is not optional.

---

## Audit section 3: Document coverage

Map documents against claims:

For each major factual claim in the current story draft or notes, ask: what document or on-record source supports this?

| Claim | Support | Status |
|---|---|---|
| [Claim 1] | Inspection report X | In hand |
| [Claim 2] | Former employee on record | Reported, waiting |
| [Claim 3] | FOIA request pending | Pending — dealbreaker if denied |
| [Claim 4] | Background source only | Gap — needs document or on-record source |

Claims with only background support are not publishable. Flag them.

---

## Audit section 4: The minimum story check

Ask: "If you stopped reporting today and had to file next week, what would you publish?"

Assess whether that story:
- Has a central claim that is documented, not inferred
- Has on-record attribution for its most important facts
- Has documented non-response or denial from the primary subject
- Is specific enough to be defended in a legal challenge

If the honest answer is "I couldn't publish anything yet," identify the single thing that would change that. That is the current critical path item.

---

## Audit section 5: Timeline pressure

Ask: "Is there a publication date? If so, what's the gap between what you need and what you have?"

If the story isn't on a hard deadline, assess whether the open-ended nature of the timeline is creating drift. Open investigations without deadlines often expand to fill all available time without converging on publishable conclusions.

Suggest a working deadline for the minimum story even if no external deadline exists.
