# Source Strategy

## Classifying sources by type and access

**Tier 1 — Primary actors**
People who made or were directly affected by the decisions at the center of the story. The most important tier. Without at least one Tier 1 source on each side of the story, it isn't fully reported.

- Decision-makers (officials, executives, contractors who made the relevant choices)
- Affected people (residents, patients, workers, families who bear the consequences)

**Tier 2 — Institutional witnesses**
People inside the institutions involved who observed but didn't make the decisions.

- Former employees (often more willing to speak than current employees)
- Whistleblowers (protected in many contexts — know the relevant law before you call)
- Colleagues and collaborators of primary actors

**Tier 3 — Expert validators**
People who can speak to the technical or policy context.

- Academic researchers, scientists, technical experts
- Lawyers and advocates working the same issue
- Former regulators who can speak to how the system should work

**Tier 4 — Record sources**
Not interviews — documents, databases, and institutional records.

- Government records (permits, inspection reports, correspondence, meeting minutes)
- Court records (filings, depositions, exhibits)
- Financial records (property records, UCC filings, SEC disclosures)
- Data sources (regulatory databases, environmental monitoring data, health records)

A story that rests entirely on Tier 3 sources is a commentary piece. Accountability stories need Tier 1.

## Approaching hostile or reluctant sources

**The non-response is also a story element.** Document every contact attempt: date, method, exact message sent or left, response received. A pattern of stonewalling, especially if documented with specifics, is publishable.

**Right of response is non-negotiable.** The primary subject of any accountability story must receive specific, detailed questions before publication and have an opportunity to respond. This is not optional. Failure to do this creates legal exposure and undercuts the story's credibility.

**How to send right-of-response questions:**
- Send in writing (email) with a stated deadline
- List specific allegations as questions, not conclusions: "Records show X. Do you dispute this? If so, what is your explanation?"
- Give a reasonable deadline (typically 48-72 hours for a non-breaking story)
- Document if they miss the deadline without responding
- If they decline to respond, document that too

**Reluctant sources** (cooperative in principle but nervous):
- Make clear what you know before asking what they know — it establishes you're serious and signals they won't be the only source
- Explain what on-record means vs. background vs. off-record before they say anything sensitive
- Don't oversell protection — be honest about what you can and can't keep confidential
- Let them tell you what they're comfortable saying; don't push them past it in the first conversation

**Anonymous sources:**
- Use only when necessary, and only when the source has direct knowledge (not second-hand)
- Know why the source needs anonymity — fear of retaliation, legal exposure, etc.
- Document the reason in your notes even if you don't publish it
- Corroborate anonymous claims with documents or additional sources wherever possible
- Describe anonymous sources as specifically as possible without revealing identity: "a person with direct knowledge of the 2019 inspection process" is more credible than "a source familiar with the matter"
