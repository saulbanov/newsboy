# Minimum and Maximum Story

The minimum/maximum frame is one of the most useful — and most abused — tools in the pitch toolkit. Done honestly, it tells the editor what they're committing to and what upside they might get. Done dishonestly, it poisons the relationship when reporting stalls.

---

## What minimum story means

The minimum story is what you can publish even if:
- The key source refuses to speak on record
- The key documents stay sealed or redacted
- The institution stonewalls all comment requests
- The investigation takes twice as long as planned

It is **not**: "We'll at least show there's a problem." That's not a story, that's a hypothesis.

It is: "Even without X, we can document Y and Z on the record, which supports the claim that [specific, bounded, publishable conclusion]."

**Test**: Read your minimum story statement and ask — would a reasonable editor, having received this story and nothing more, publish it? If the honest answer is no, the minimum story isn't real yet.

---

## What maximum story means

The maximum story is what a fully-reported version looks like if everything comes together. It should be ambitious but mechanically plausible — the chain of reporting steps from current state to maximum story should be traceable.

**Common oversells to watch for**:

- "This could lead to policy change." Policy change requires a mechanism. Name it: which specific regulator, legislator, or court would act, and on what timeline?
- "This could expose a pattern across the state/country." Patterns require evidence from multiple instances. Do you have leads on those instances, or are you projecting?
- "This is a Pulitzer story." Don't say this. It makes editors suspicious.
- "This could go national." It could. But why would a national outlet pick it up? Is there a specific editor or outlet interested?

A strong maximum story statement sounds like: "If we get the documents under FOIA and find what we expect to find, this is a story about a specific regulatory decision that affected X number of people, with named officials who can be held accountable. That's a story an editor could submit for [specific award or context]."

---

## The gap between minimum and maximum

The gap between them is your reporting risk profile. A small gap means the story is solid but may not be transformative. A large gap means high upside but high uncertainty.

Be explicit about which key variables determine whether you get to maximum:
- "If the former inspector agrees to go on record, we can name the specific decision-maker"
- "If the FOIA comes through with the inspection reports, we can document the timeline"
- "If the plaintiff's attorney shares the test results, we can prove exposure levels"

This turns the gap from a vague worry into a specific set of reporting tasks — which is what an editor needs to decide whether to assign the story.
