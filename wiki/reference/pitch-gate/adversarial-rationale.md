# Adversarial Posture — Why the Editor Says No by Default

The pitch gate is the cheapest failure point in the pipeline. A story that fails here fails in ten minutes. A story that fails after a week of reporting wastes a week of reporting.

The adversarial posture — assume the story does not exist until demonstrated — is not hostility. It is protection. The reporter who most wants the story commissioned is the worst judge of whether it should be. The editor's skepticism performs a function the reporter cannot perform on their own material.

---

## Why the default is no, not maybe

A "maybe" verdict is a soft commission. The reporter hears "maybe" as "probably yes if I keep working." They commit time. They make source calls. They file FOIAs. They arrive at the next gate with a story they're attached to — which is the worst time to ask whether it should exist.

A genuine "needs more" verdict identifies the exact gap that must be closed before any reporting resources are committed. It is not an invitation to keep working. It is a hold until a specific condition is met.

The adversarial posture enforces this distinction by making "no" the starting position. The reporter must move the editor off no. If they can't, the story isn't ready.

---

## What the adversarial posture is not

**Not hostile.** The goal is not to defeat the pitch — it's to surface the weakest links before they are tested in the field. A story that survives the adversarial posture is a story worth pursuing.

**Not generic skepticism.** "This seems thin" is not a verdict. The adversarial posture requires specific engagement: what would have to be true for this to be publishable? Is that true? If not, what's missing?

**Not a substitute for judgment.** The adversarial posture is a starting position, not a fixed position. Evidence moves the editor. The question is: does the evidence presented actually move it?

---

## The two failure modes the posture is designed to catch

**The exciting unprovable claim:** A single tantalizing finding that hasn't been independently verified and may not hold up. The reporter is excited; the foundation is thin. The adversarial posture asks: what is the minimum evidence required to publish this? Does it currently exist?

**The improperly focused theme statement:** The story may be real but framed so broadly it can never converge. The adversarial posture asks: can the central claim be documented with specific, obtainable evidence? Or does proving it require proving everything at once?

These are the two ways a story can fail at the pitch gate without being a bad story. Both are fixable. Neither is fixable without first being named.
