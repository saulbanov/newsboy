# Confidence Guide — Hypothesis Confidence Levels

Confidence reflects the **chain**, not the evidence. High confidence means: if this hypothesis is true, the evidence should be unambiguous and locatable. Low confidence means: even if you find evidence, you may not be sure what it proves.

This distinction matters: a Tier 1 hypothesis with low confidence is still worth running — it's cheap. A Tier 2 hypothesis with low confidence should be parked until a Tier 1 signal supports it.

---

## High confidence

The cause-effect relationship is strongly implied by established facts. The evidence path is clear and the evidence, if it exists, will be unambiguous.

**Signs of high confidence:**
- The claim follows directly from documented facts — minimal inferential steps
- The evidence type is well-defined and unlikely to be ambiguous
- Disconfirmation would be equally clear (absence of evidence in a known location is informative)

**Example:** "CME surveillance obligations cover trades of the documented size — the rulebook either has threshold language or it doesn't." The claim is specific, the evidence location is known, and the result will be unambiguous.

---

## Medium confidence

The claim is plausible but depends on assumptions. Evidence may be partial, require interpretation, or point in more than one direction.

**Signs of medium confidence:**
- The relationship requires 2-3 inferential steps from established facts
- The evidence may be ambiguous or interpretable multiple ways
- Confirmation is possible but partial confirmation is the likely outcome

**Example:** "Congressional pressure is building beyond Murphy." Plausible given Murphy's statement, but other members' private positions are unknown. A machine search will find some signal but not the full picture.

---

## Low confidence

Speculative. The chain is logically possible but several steps removed from what's established. Evidence may not exist, or may be present but uninterpretable without additional context.

**Signs of low confidence:**
- Requires assuming something is true that hasn't been established
- Evidence path is unclear or depends on non-public records being accessible
- Even positive evidence would be ambiguous

**Example:** "Specific named pension funds can be identified as counterparties." The aggregate harm is certain; the named harm is speculative without non-public clearing data.

---

## The named vs. aggregate distinction

Some hypotheses are high-confidence at the aggregate level but low-confidence at the specific level. Label this explicitly:

> "Confidence: Low on *named institution* claim. The mathematical existence of counterparties is certain (High), but identifying specific named institutions requires non-public clearing data (Low). Confidence label reflects the more specific, publishable claim."

This matters for commissioning: if unnamed aggregate harm is sufficient for the story, the confidence rises to medium or high.

---

## Common calibration errors

**Overconfidence from secondary sources:** WSJ reported it → it must be true. Secondary sources are not evidence. The hypothesis must be testable against primary sources.

**Underconfidence from absence of evidence:** "I couldn't find anything, so the evidence might not exist." Absence in a search is not absence in the record. Note what was searched; flag for more targeted search before lowering confidence.

**Confidence conflated with newsworthiness:** A low-confidence hypothesis can still be a high-value story if confirmed. These are separate evaluations. Pitch-gate evaluates newsworthiness; hypothesis formation evaluates testability and confidence.
