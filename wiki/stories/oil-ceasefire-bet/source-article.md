# Source Article — $950M Bearish Oil Bet Placed Hours Before Trump's Iran Ceasefire

Published: Apr 8, 2026
Source: finance.yahoo.com / Reuters / LSEG data

---

Traders placed an approximately $950 million bearish bet on falling oil prices just hours before President Donald Trump announced a two-week ceasefire with Iran on Tuesday evening, according to Reuters, citing LSEG data. The trade, involving roughly 8,600 combined lots of Brent and WTI crude futures sold around 1945 GMT, came approximately three hours before Trump's ceasefire declaration at around 2230 GMT — and roughly two hours before his self-imposed 8 p.m. Eastern deadline for Iran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz.

Crude futures subsequently plunged around 15% to 19%, with WTI falling to near $91 a barrel — its steepest intraday decline since 2020 — while Brent dropped below $95.

## A Familiar Pattern

The trade bears a striking resemblance to a suspicious $580 million oil futures sale that took place on March 23, roughly 15 minutes before Trump posted on Truth Social that the U.S. had been engaged in "productive conversations" with Iran. In that instance, about 6,200 Brent and WTI contracts changed hands in a single minute between 6:49 and 6:50 a.m. New York time, according to the Financial Times' analysis of Bloomberg data. Oil prices then crashed as much as 14%, benefiting whoever had positioned for the decline.

Those March trades triggered calls for investigation from lawmakers including Senator Chris Murphy, who labeled the timing "mindblowing corruption". The Wall Street Journal documented a broader pattern of well-timed trades occurring moments before Trump's policy surprises. The White House denied any profiteering at the time.

## Ceasefire Brings Relief, Uncertainty Remains

Trump's ceasefire announcement, posted on Truth Social about 90 minutes before his deadline, suspended the U.S. bombing campaign against Iran for two weeks. Iran's Supreme National Security Council accepted the terms, and its foreign minister said passage through the Strait of Hormuz — which carries roughly 20% of global oil supply — would be permitted under Iranian military management.

Markets rallied broadly. The S&P 500 leaped 2.1%, the Dow Jones Industrial Average surged over 1,000 points, and Asian markets including Japan's Nikkei 225 rose more than 4%. Yet shipping companies remained cautious on Wednesday, with most stranded tankers still inside the Gulf as operators awaited clarity on safe-passage logistics.

Despite the drop, oil remains well above pre-war levels. Analysts at Commerzbank warned that "market participants are still apprehensive, as the situation post the 14 days remains uncertain".
